# Spine Options

Five candidate narrative spines. These are not five phrasings of one idea — each is a different **engine**, with a different antagonist, a different location of cost, and different structural consequences.

Context: the current spine (`research/spine.md`) states a theme rather than an engine. Nobody wants anything, nobody opposes anybody, and the four endings loosen the vise that the resolution principle says must remain. Each option below is an attempt to fix that.

---

## 1. The Unsafe Room

Four executives meet as strangers in a hotel and, for the first time in years, say something true to another person. On the second day each of them discovers that one of the others is about to hold power over them — a board seat, a contract, a hiring decision — and that the confession has become leverage. Each must choose whether to carry the honest self back into the room where it can be used, while the person who saw them watches.

- **Antagonist:** hierarchy, reasserting itself.
- **Buys:** the final honest act costs something; the hotel stops being a safe room, which is the point of the whole premise.
- **Costs:** nearly nothing. The entanglements already exist in the draft (Michael on Kate's board, Alex's product sold to Michael's bank) — they simply move from the epilogue into the two days.

---

## 2. The Fall

Nik arrives for a chess tournament with a lie collapsing behind him — the studio is not wobbling, it is dying, and he is the only one who knows. Over two days he tells three strangers the truth in pieces, and on the last morning the news reaches the lobby in front of them. Each of the other three can help him, and each can only do it by doing the exact thing their own armor was built to prevent.

- **Antagonist:** exposure.
- **Buys:** a real event in the middle of the book; the four honest moves become actions under pressure rather than insights; the ensemble converges on one crisis.
- **Costs:** tips into caper if the help gets clever. Nik carries the book.

---

## 3. Michael's Clock

Michael comes to the hotel to give a valedictory speech at a conference he no longer believes in. Over two days he charms three strangers into telling him the truth, gives each of them the advice that would have saved his own life, and cannot take a word of it himself. The only one who understands what she has just watched is Kate, who is twenty-five years behind him on the same road.

- **Antagonist:** time.
- **Buys:** Michael becomes the warning he is supposed to be (`book_draft.md:94` — "Michael is Kate's possible future"), which the current happy ending destroys. The book earns its warmth elsewhere.
- **Costs:** this is the hard-understatement version. Michael pays and does not recover.

---

## 4. The Pact

On the second night four strangers discover the narcotic relief of confessing to people who cannot hurt them, and they keep going well past the point of safety. They leave the hotel each holding something that could end one of the others, and an unspoken agreement never to use it. The novel is the year that follows, when the world comes for each of them in turn and the agreement is all they have.

- **Antagonist:** each other.
- **Buys:** Part Three becomes the book instead of an epilogue, which fixes the proportions problem.
- **Costs:** the hotel is no longer the center; the "Grand Hotel" structural hook is largely surrendered.

---

## 5. Good Advice

Four executives spend two days giving one another the counsel their own worlds could never give them, and every word of it is right. They go home, act on it, and it doesn't save them — because the advice was aimed at the wound, and the vise was always real. What they are left with is not a solution but each other: the only four people alive who know.

- **Antagonist:** the world, proving them right.
- **Buys:** maximum credibility with the target reader; the only ending that honors the stated resolution principle (`spine.md:59` — the ending should not remove the vise).
- **Costs:** the hardest book to sell. Requires the four to keep speaking after the hotel.

---

## Recommendation

**Option 1 is the chassis; Option 5 is the ending it is reaching for.**

The unsafe room gives a plot that costs nothing to build — the entanglements are already written, just relocated. And "good advice that doesn't save anyone, but leaves you known" is the only resolution that squares `spine.md:59` with what actually happens to the four of them.

**Option 3** is the one to reach for if Fork B (`analysis_format_genre.md:121`) resolves toward hard understatement.

## Open dependencies

These options cannot be chosen independently of the open forks:

- **Fork B** (warm resolution vs. hard understatement) must be settled *before* the spine, not after. A warm resolution and a hard one are two different spines.
- **Fork C** (bright / bitter / mixed endings) is largely decided by whichever option is chosen: 1 and 4 permit mixed; 3 and 5 require someone to pay.
