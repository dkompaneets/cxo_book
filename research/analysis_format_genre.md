# Analysis: Format, Genre, Tone, Comps

A working document on the strategic decisions around the novel about four CXOs. Separate from the story file — this is about **how** to make it, not **what** happens in it.

---

## The goal we're optimizing for

The primary criterion is **maximum credibility with actual CXOs** ("yes, this is exactly it, the author has been inside") plus a shot at breakout. This is a cynical, time-poor audience with a fine nose for falseness. Credibility is bought with precision of detail, the absence of overt moralizing, and the recognition of the unspoken. It's destroyed by sentimentality, preaching, and tidy happy endings.

The core credibility asset is the concept itself — **the "vise."** It's precise and usually goes unspoken. As long as the book names it and shows it from the inside without gloss, it lands. The moment it starts to comfort and moralize, it loses the audience.

---

## Axis 1. Type of text (fiction ↔ nonfiction)

Decided before genre.

**1. Pure fiction**
+ Full freedom of plot, arcs, endings; no legal risk; drama can be tuned to perfection.
− Loses the power of "this is true"; the reader keeps a distance; harder to sell as "insider."

**2. Narrative nonfiction (real stories written like a novel)** — Michael Lewis
+ Maximum authenticity; reads like a novel but "it's all true"; prestige.
− Requires real reporting (interviews, consents, verifiability); you can't invent interior life or endings; access to CXO candor is limited; legal risk.

**3. Autofiction**
+ The "composite of real people" method is built in; intellectual prestige.
− Cold, cerebral, thin on plot; conflicts with warmth and redemptive arcs; niche audience; hard for a debut.

**4. Roman à clef ("novel with a key")**
+ The protection of fiction + the juice of "this is about real people"; gossip pull.
− Highest legal risk (defamation if anyone is recognizable); can damage relationships; the effect only works if the reader knows the prototypes.

**5. Research-backed fiction (invention grounded in real research / knowledge of the milieu)**
+ Freedom of invention + truth of detail; legally safe; authenticity without the obligations of reporting; endings shaped freely. **This is the project's originally described method.**
− Needs research / insider knowledge for the details to ring true; a skeptic still partly discounts "it's fiction."

**6. Essayistic / nonfiction reflection**
+ A clear "takeaway"; plays to the strength of the concept.
− Kills the characters, scenes, emotion — that's a different book.

**7. Hybrid: fiction + documentary interludes**
+ Marries the emotion of story with the authority of fact; interludes are a home for analysis and data; interludes can be repurposed as standalone pieces; differentiates the book.
− Harder to position ("is it a novel or nonfiction?"); the rhythm breaks; risky for a debut.

**8. Docufiction / verbatim**
+ Raw truth of the voices; methodologically fresh.
− Needs a large corpus of real interviews and consents; technically hard; exotic for the market.

## Axis 2. Genre / style (within fiction)

**1. Upmarket / book-club literature of converging lives** (Backman, Towles, Strout)
+ Fits the material perfectly (warmth + depth + arcs); loved by clubs; allows humor.
− Crowded genre; needs excellent prose; "quiet" books struggle to break through.

**2. Grand Hotel novel (the hotel as vessel)** (Vicki Baum, Towles)
+ A ready-made structural hook ("the hotel novel"); enclosed space, unity of place; strong sense of place.
− The "before" and "after" parts break the hotel's unity; risk of staticness.

**3. Corporate satire / workplace comedy** ("Succession," Ferris)
+ Humor gets a home; sharp topicality.
− Cold; undermines the main goal ("the human behind the title"); risk of caricature; dates quickly.

**4. Tragicomedy / comic literary novel** (Greer's "Less," Tropper, Semple)
+ Hits the target tone precisely ("warm + funny, not mocking"); pain and comedy coexist; humor humanizes. **The most natural home for the intended tone.**
− The hardest tone to sustain; two co-authors must sync their comic voice.

**5. Business novel / leadership parable** (Lencioni, Goldratt)
+ A huge ready market; a clear "takeaway"; commercially predictable.
− Literary quality is sacrificed to the lesson; interiority is flattened; risk of preachiness; the nuance of self-constructed loneliness is lost.

**6. Linked short-story cycle** (Strout, Egan)
+ Matches the "four portraits" structure; each gets a complete story; prestige; stories can be published separately.
− Collections sell worse than novels; the "convergence" is looser; less drive.

**7. Multi-POV "converging strangers" (commercial upmarket)** (Moriarty)
+ A proven hit structure; high breakout ceiling; adaptation potential; book-club catnip.
− Pulls toward a twist/melodrama and away from quiet interiority; usually needs a "hook" event.

**8. Autofiction of confession** (Cusk)
+ The "people confess to a listener" model fits the hotel; prestige.
− Cold, thin on plot; bright endings feel foreign; niche.

**9. Big social novel** (Franzen, Wolfe)
+ Scale + humor + social commentary + interiority together.
− Extremely hard to pull off in a debut; long; needs virtuoso prose.

---

## Top 3 formats (both axes combined)

**1. Research-backed fiction in a tragicomic mode ("A Novel") — THE CHOICE**
+ The original method; freedom of arcs and endings; legally safe; humor gets a home.
− Needs research for plausibility; a difficult tone for two authors.

**2. Hybrid: fiction + documentary interludes**
+ Emotion + authority of fact; interludes differentiate and get reused.
− Harder to position; broken rhythm; risky for a debut.

**3. Business parable / leadership novel**
+ A clear "takeaway"; predictable commercial path.
− Flattens interiority into "leadership lessons"; preachiness.

**Why the choice is #1:** everything built into the story (fine wounds, intersections, warm humor, redemptive arcs) can only live, artistically, here. Strict nonfiction forbids inventing interior life and endings; satire and autofiction are too cold; the business parable kills interiority. #1 holds all of it without loss.

**Caveat:** #1 is stronger artistically; #2 and #3 are stronger on recognizability with a business audience. A pragmatic compromise — write it as #1, and optionally add a light layer of interludes from #2 without turning the book into didactics.

## Open forks (to decide before writing)

### Fork A. Whether to rely on real CXO interviews
- **For:** raises authenticity and the breakout ceiling; real details you can't invent; moves closer to narrative nonfiction.
- **Against:** requires time, consents, and access to candor (CXOs rarely open up to the needed depth); legal/reputational risk where anyone is recognizable; **not required** — research-backed fiction can ring true on accumulated insider knowledge of the milieu, without formal interviews.
- **A strong alternative to interviews — mining public accounts.** The internet is full of first-hand executive testimony about exactly this "vise" (loneliness at the top, the ban on showing weakness, being caught between board and team, the filtered information, the family cost). These can be aggregated at scale, and where a **repeating pattern** emerges across many independent sources, that pattern itself becomes the evidence base — the proof that the book's premise is real. This grounds the fiction in documented reality, sharpens which wounds are archetypal vs. idiosyncratic, and can largely remove the need for original interviews. (Documented examples already exist: HBR/RHR data that ~half of CEOs report loneliness and 61% say it hurts their performance; first-hand accounts of "you become a title, not a person"; records of the disengaged executive whose reports had to route through the CFO; rising board-driven CEO turnover; the specific, deeper isolation reported by women executives.)
- **Status:** open. Format #2 (research-backed fiction) is compatible with all of the above: interviews — and public-account mining — are levers *within* the format, not separate formats.

### Fork B. Tone of resolution: warmth vs. hard understatement
- **Warm resolution:** inspiring, book-club-friendly. Risk: for a CXO audience, tidy happy endings back-to-back read as "the author was never inside."
- **Hard understatement:** maximum authenticity; the vise stays a vise, only how the person breathes inside it changes ("it didn't get easier, but I'm not alone in it"). Risk: bleakness, nothing for the reader to hold onto.
- **Status:** open. Directly affects credibility with the target audience — probably more important than Fork A.

### Fork C. The characters' fates in the finale
- **All toward the light** — warm but predictable by the third character; risk of sentimentality.
- **All toward the bitter** — honest and memorable, but hard to sell widely.
- **Different endings (mixed)** — the most plausible (not everyone makes it); the arcs stop being copies; warmth and honesty coexist. Downside: harder to balance so it doesn't read mechanically as "two won, two lost."
- **Status:** open. Overlaps with Fork B.

## Comps: three groups × three books

### Group 1. Literary ensemble of converging lives (our choice)

**Nine Perfect Strangers — Liane Moriarty.** Nine strangers at a ten-day retreat, each with a wound; their stories and coping mechanisms surface gradually. *Similar:* almost our structure — strangers in one place, alternating POV, "the person behind the facade"; essentially five or six parallel stories in one location. *Different:* a thriller hook (secret LSD micro-dosing); the characters are a broad cross-section, not executives.

**A Man Called Ove — Fredrik Backman.** Behind the prickly mask of a grumpy widower opens a broken, deeply feeling man; warmth and humor mixed with pain. *Similar:* the core — showing the living person behind the facade; humor humanizes. *Different:* a single protagonist, not a corporate world.

**Less — Andrew Sean Greer (Pulitzer).** A comic literary novel: a hapless writer flees his life on a round-the-world trip; funny and tender at once. *Similar:* the benchmark for the target tone — "funny and tender," rich interior life. *Different:* a single protagonist, a travel picaresque, a different subject.

### Group 2. Business parable / leadership novel

**The Five Dysfunctions of a Team — Patrick Lencioni.** A new CEO of a stalling company unites a dysfunctional executive team. *Similar:* fictional C-suite, recognizable types. *Different:* equal parts fable and handbook — an explicit model follows the story; the characters serve the lesson; no interiority.

**The Goal — Eliyahu Goldratt.** A plant manager saves a factory in 90 days while learning the "theory of constraints." *Similar:* the novel as a vehicle for teaching thinking; a protagonist under pressure. *Different:* a focus on methodology; the plot in service of the lesson; no warmth.

**The Phoenix Project — Gene Kim et al.** An IT manager rescues a failing project; the novel teaches DevOps. *Similar:* corporate crisis, fictional executives. *Different:* heavily technical, didactic.

*(This group shows the risk: once the lesson comes to the fore, "the human behind the title" is flattened.)*

### Group 3. Narrative nonfiction (maximum credibility with CXOs)

**Liar's Poker — Michael Lewis.** Insider nonfiction about 1980s Wall Street culture, written like a novel. *Similar:* shows the living person and the absurdity behind the financial titles; wit. *Different:* it's truth/memoir, the author is a participant; you can't invent interior life or endings.

**Barbarians at the Gate — Burrough & Helyar.** A documentary chronicle of the RJR Nabisco buyout, reading like a thriller about a boardroom war of egos. *Similar:* executives under pressure, boardroom wars, the vise of interests. *Different:* real people and events; a focus on a single deal.

**The Hard Thing About Hard Things — Ben Horowitz.** A CEO memoir on the brutal reality of running a company and the loneliness at the top. *Similar:* directly about the "vise" and the cost of leadership, an insider voice. *Different:* a memoir with advice, first person, nonfiction.

### How to read them together
The three groups are three different books from the same material. Group 1 is what's intended (warmth + depth, freedom of arcs). Group 2 prioritizes recognizability with a business audience (at the cost of interiority). Group 3 prioritizes CXO credibility (at the cost of inventive freedom and warm endings). The nearest single reference point for the concept: **Nine Perfect Strangers for structure + the tone of Less + the heart of A Man Called Ove.**

## Status summary

- **Type of text:** research-backed fiction (#5), flexible — compatible with interviews, with public-account mining, or with neither.
- **Genre/tone:** tragicomedy / warm literary ensemble of converging lives.
- **Open:** (A) CXO interviews — yes/no, with public-account mining as a strong evidence-gathering alternative; (B) tone of resolution — warm/hard; (C) endings — bright/bitter/mixed.
- **Comp reference:** Nine Perfect Strangers × Less × A Man Called Ove.
