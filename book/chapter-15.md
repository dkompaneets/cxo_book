# 15. Version 4 (Final) (Final v2)

At 8:49 on the Sunday morning Kate Merrick opened the folder to put the deck on the lectern machine, and the deck was wrong.

She knew it in about a second and a half, from the third slide, before she had read a word of it: the third slide was the one with the market map on it, and the market map on the screen had four boxes and the market map she had built on Wednesday had six, and the two extra boxes were the entire argument.

She scrolled.

Slide seven had the old growth bridge. Slide eleven had a table of Refill assumptions with an August launch in it. Slide fourteen — the one that mattered, the one she had rebuilt four times, the one Michael had unlocked for her in a bar on Thursday night, the one that said *since March we have been running a piece of work to establish whether twenty-two was recoverable, and here is what it cost us to find out, and here is what we found instead* — slide fourteen did not exist. In its place was a slide with a chart on it and the title *FY26 — Path to Target.*

It was the version from the second of July.

Eleven minutes.

---

The first thing she felt was not panic. It was a kind of clean bright fury, and it had a name in it.

*Dawn.*

She had sent the final on Wednesday night at 11:40. She had sent it with the words *this is the one — please make sure this is what's in the board folder and kill the others.* She could see her own message. She could see it with total clarity, the way you can see anything at 8:49 on a Sunday morning eleven minutes before a board.

She rang.

It went to voicemail on the second ring, which meant the phone had been declined, which meant — and Kate's mind produced this instantly, with the wrong emphasis on every word — *she's asleep.*

She rang again. Voicemail again.

And then, in the way these things arrive, in the exact worst order, the memory came up: Tuesday, the corridor by the lifts, Dawn with her coat half on and a phone in her hand, saying something. Kate had been walking and reading. Dawn had said — she had actually said it, the words were there, they had been sitting there all week in a drawer — *"So I'm going to be off Sunday, Marcus has got the thing with his chest again, they've said to keep him home and Steve's away, so I won't be reachable, is that going to be—"*

And Kate, walking, reading, had said: "Course. Fine. Do what you need to."

She had half-heard it between two other things. She had heard it and filed it and it had not survived contact with Thursday.

Nine minutes.

---

There was nobody to call.

That is a sentence that means nothing to most people and everything to about four hundred thousand people in the country, and Kate stood in the corridor behind the Warwick Suite — the service corridor, grey carpet tiles, a stacked trolley of chairs, a fire door — and went through it properly, because she was a person who went through things properly.

Gareth had the file. Gareth was in London. Gareth would take four minutes to answer on a Sunday and would then need to be told where to look, and the file was in a personal drive, and he did not have access to the personal drive, and setting up access to the personal drive required — she genuinely thought this through, to the end — Dawn.

Tom did not have it. Tom had never had it.

The board were in the room. Harriet was in the room; she had seen her go in at 8:44 with a coffee.

There was a version of this in which Kate walked in at nine and said *the wrong file's in the folder, give me fifteen minutes,* and it was, objectively, entirely survivable, and would have been forgotten by Tuesday.

She knew that. She knew it *at the time*. She stood in a service corridor and knew that the reasonable move was to walk in and say a true sentence, and she could not do it, because seven people were about to be told that a number she had personally put in front of them for four consecutive years was wrong, and the only asset she had in that room was that she was the person who did not drop things.

Seven minutes.

And that was when it started.

---

It came up from the feet. That was the thing nobody had told her.

Her hands went first — not shaking, but *gone*, a sort of hot absence in them — and then her chest closed by about a third, and then her vision did something at the edges, and her heart went at a rate that she noted, with the part of her that never stopped taking minutes, was well over a hundred and forty.

She got a hand on the trolley of stacked chairs.

She had done fourteen-hour days for eleven years. She had done a fire in a warehouse. She had done the week in 2022 when the funding fell over. She had never once, not for a second, had her body simply refuse, and the horror of it was not the symptoms, it was the *insubordination* — the discovery that the thing she had been driving for twenty years had a view.

She got her breath by counting the carpet tiles, which was not a technique, it was just what was there.

And then her hands, which had been doing this ten thousand times, did the thing they did.

They took out the phone. They found the name. They pressed and held.

---

She spoke for ninety-one seconds.

She did not shout. That was what would matter later; that was what made it what it was. If she had shouted it would have been a bad moment from a woman under pressure, and Dawn would have forgiven it before it finished playing.

Kate Merrick's voice, on the recording, is perfectly level. It is warm at the start. It is the voice she uses on stage.

"Dawn — it's me. It's eight fifty-two on Sunday. I'm standing outside the board and the deck in the folder is the second of July version."

A pause of two seconds.

"I need you to understand what that means, so I'm going to say it once. Slide fourteen isn't in it. I built slide fourteen over four days last week and it is the only reason this meeting doesn't end my career, and I sent it to you on Wednesday night with a message that said, and I'm reading it, *please make sure this is what's in the board folder and kill the others.* And you've either not read it or not done it, and either way I've got — " a breath " — six minutes.

"And I can't call you, because you're not available, because I'm apparently the only person in this company for whom Sunday is a working day.

"I have never once said this to you, so I'm going to say it now. There is a difference between a job and a role. A job is a list of things and you do them and you go home at five with your tupperware and your shoebox. A role is where you are accountable for an outcome and you carry it, and it does not stop at five, and I have been carrying yours for two years and pretending it was mine.

"I'm going to go into this room now and do the best forty minutes of my life from memory. And when I come out we are going to have a completely different conversation about what you are for.

"Do not ring me back."

She stopped the recording. She looked at it for one second. She sent it.

Then she put the phone in her jacket, and straightened her jacket, and walked in at 8:59.

---

She was magnificent.

There is no other word and there were seven witnesses. She stood at the front of the Warwick Suite with the wrong deck on the screen behind her and she said, at 9:01, "You've got a version in front of you that's three weeks old. Ignore it. I'll send the right one this afternoon. I'd rather talk than click."

And then she did it from memory. Forty-four minutes. She rebuilt the market map on the flip chart with a marker in four minutes, six boxes, and it was better on the flip chart than it had been on the slide because you could watch it being built. She did the growth bridge as numbers spoken out loud, in order, and when Simon from the fund asked for the September Refill sensitivity she gave him three scenarios to one decimal place without notes.

And at 9:26 she said the sentence.

"Since March we've been running a piece of work to find out whether twenty-two was recoverable. It isn't. I want to take you through what it cost us to find that out, and what we found instead."

Nobody moved.

At 9:31 Harriet Nash said, "Since March," in a voice with nothing in it.

And Kate said — calmly, having been told the answer by an old man in a bar on Thursday, and having had thirty-six hours to make it hers — "Yes. I could have brought you a worry in March. I brought you a finding in July. The interval is mine and I'll own it, and if you'd rather have the worry next time, tell me now and you'll get it, and I'd advise against it."

Harriet looked at her for four seconds.

"No," she said. "You're right."

---

At 10:40 they broke for coffee.

Kate went out into the corridor with the good, high, floating feeling of a fight won, and got her laptop off the side table, and thought: *right, send them the actual deck.*

She opened the drive.

She navigated: Board / FY26 / Summit / Final.

There were four files in it.

*FY26 Board — v3.pptx*
*FY26 Board — v4 FINAL.pptx*
*FY26 Board — v4 FINAL v2.pptx*
*FY26 Board — v4 FINAL v2 (KM edits).pptx*

She opened the last one. Slide fourteen. Six boxes. Every number she had built on Wednesday night, exactly where she had left it, timestamped 23:41 on Wednesday.

She sat down slowly on the edge of the side table.

Then she opened her sent messages, and read the one from Wednesday night, and read it again, and then a third time, very slowly, the way you read a word that has stopped meaning anything.

*Dawn — this is the one, please make sure this is what's in the board folder and kill the others.*

Under it, at 23:42, Dawn's reply, which Kate had not opened at the time and had not opened since:

*Will do first thing. Just so you know I can't get into your personal drive (never could) so I can only put in what you drop into the shared one. Have dropped in what's there now, shout if it's not the right one.*

And under that, at 23:44, from Kate herself — one word, sent from a bed, at the end of a fourteen-hour day, in the four seconds it took to reach across and turn out a light:

*Perfect.*

---

She had dropped v4 FINAL into the shared folder on the second of July.

She had built v4 FINAL v2 (KM edits) on the Wednesday, in her own drive, at 11:41 at night, and had saved it, and had told a woman with no access to that drive to make sure it was the one in the folder, and had then read a reply saying *I can only put in what you drop into the shared one* and had typed the word *Perfect.*

There was no error. There was no forgetting, no dropped ball, no assistant who did not care. There was a woman who had done exactly what she said she would do, who had flagged the precise risk in writing at 23:42 on a Wednesday, and who had then, on a Sunday morning, with a sick seven-year-old on a sofa and her husband away, received ninety-one seconds of the calmest voice in England explaining that she did not understand the difference between a job and a role.

Kate sat on the side table in the corridor of the Warwick Suite.

She opened the phone. She went to the message. She looked at it for a long time — at the little grey icon, the waveform, the *1:31* — and at the word underneath it, which said *Delivered*, and, below that, in smaller grey letters, *Played 09:14.*

Played at 9:14. On a Sunday morning. Fifteen minutes after Kate walked into the room.

There was no button. She looked for one anyway, the way everybody does, going through the menus of an application she had used four thousand times, looking for a thing she knew perfectly well was not there.

"Kate?" said Harriet Nash from the doorway. "They're ready when you are. That was really excellent, by the way."
