# 6. Check-In

The Ardenne was built in 1974 by a consortium that went bankrupt in 1976, and its architecture is therefore a complete and honest record of a mood that lasted about eighteen months. A twelve-storey slab of bedrooms; a low podium wrapped round it containing everything else; and a porte-cochère held up on four concrete columns of a girth suggesting the architect expected weather from below.

It was refurbished in 1998, and again in 2016. The 2016 refurbishment installed, in the lobby, a fireplace that does not work, an installation of nine hundred glass rods, and a smell.

On the Thursday it was hosting three things at once.

In the Warwick Suite: the Bellwether strategy summit, sixty-one delegates, private, with its own coffee and its own lanyards in a colour the events team described as *fern*.

In Halls 2 and 3: the Banking Operations Forum, four days, four hundred delegates, twenty-two exhibition stands, of which twelve were selling essentially the same fraud product.

And in the atrium, on eighty-eight trestle tables hired from a firm in Coleshill: the Midlands Open, seven rounds, a hundred and seventy-six players, an easel saying SILENCE PLEASE — GAMES IN PROGRESS, and a man on the door with a metal cash tin.

The events team had raised the collision with the general manager in April, and the general manager had said the thing general managers say, which was that the building had four separate circulations. This was true on the drawings.

---

Nik arrived first, at 1:40, pulling the maroon case, which meant walking with a slight lean, like a man in a headwind.

Check-in took nine minutes because the woman ahead of him was disputing a charge from 2023 with the stamina of someone who has retired and found a purpose. Nik stood behind her with the case against his leg and read the tariff card in its little perspex holder, which he had not looked at when he booked.

Standard double: £141. Per night. Five nights.

Seven hundred and five pounds. He had known that; he had paid it. What happened at the desk was not the discovery of a number but the arrival of one number in the same room as another, which is an entirely different event, and the other number was £1,140, due on the first of August. He did the division before he could stop himself. Five nights here was two-thirds of a month of his daughter's school.

"Sir? Sorry to keep you."

"Not at all," said Nik warmly.

"Just the one guest? And it's — Nik-heel? Sorry."

"Nikhil," said Nik. And then, because it had been twenty-six years: "Nik's fine."

"Lovely. You're on eight, chess is through the double doors past the fireplace. Do you need a lanyard?"

"There are lanyards?"

"There are *three sorts* of lanyards," she said, with feeling.

---

Kate arrived at 2:15 and was still talking when the car door opened.

"—so tell Tom the answer's yes but he has to write down what happens in November, not what happens in August, and if he says the word 'directionally' I want you to laugh at him, out loud, on the call, as me—"

She came through the revolving door at a speed that made the doorman step back, phone at her collarbone, bag sliding.

"—and I'll have signal all weekend, so if it's genuinely urgent, genuinely, then ring. But Dawn, I mean *genuinely*—"

The clerk had a form and a pen. Kate took the pen without breaking stride, in the hand not holding the phone, began to fill in a car registration she did not know, stopped, and made a gesture of such precise and universal meaning — *one moment, I am very sorry, I am aware of how this looks* — that the clerk, who was twenty-three, felt obscurely flattered.

"Right. I'm at the hotel. I'm going to go and be strategic at people. Love you, bye." She sent it and put the phone face down on the marble, which lasted four seconds. "Sorry. Merrick. M-E-double-R-I-C-K."

"You're the fern lanyard."

"I'm the what?"

"Bellwether. Fern. You're on eleven, there's a thing in the bathroom, I'll let you find it." He slid the card over. "And your team have the Warwick Suite from four. And — sorry, there's a note — a Ms Nash asks whether you can do a call at six rather than seven."

Kate looked at the note.

"Of course she does," she said pleasantly, and picked the phone back up.

---

Alex arrived at 4:50 and did not speak to a human being for eleven minutes.

He had the big headphones on, and had not been listening to anything for the last half hour of the train, because he had been thinking about a problem, and the headphones had simply stayed on as a wall rather than a speaker.

He joined the wrong queue twice. First the atrium, which turned out to be chess registration, where a man with a cash tin said something Alex could not hear and did not remove the headphones for. Then the Banking Operations Forum desk, where a woman said, brightly, "Have we got you on the delegate list?"

"I'm — no. Sorry. I'm a hotel."

"You're a hotel?"

"I'm *staying* in the hotel," said Alex, going red, and left.

At the actual desk he gave his name and had to give it twice. "Voy-chick. It's a W. It's spelled with a W but it's a V." A sentence he had said perhaps nine hundred times, and which came out, as always, slightly too fast.

"And is it just yourself?"

"My parents come Saturday. Different booking. Wójcik as well. Also with a W."

"Aw," said the clerk. "Family weekend."

And Alex — who had come for five days because it was ninety minutes from London and because if he stayed in the flat he would go to the office, and who had told his parents this was a break he'd been planning for months — said, "Yeah."

He went up to the ninth floor, put the bag down, opened the laptop, connected to the wifi, tested the latency, sighed at it, and started working.

---

Michael arrived at 5:20 and was, within ninety seconds, on excellent terms with everyone behind the desk.

"Right. I'm going to warn you now, my name's Halloway and there'll be two of me on that system, because I booked it myself, badly, and then the bank's travel department booked it again, properly, and the bank's version has me arriving yesterday."

"Ah," said the clerk, whose badge said FOLA. "Yes. There are two of you."

"There are. One of them has been here since Wednesday having a lovely time and the other one is me."

"Do you want me to cancel the—"

"No, no. Leave him. He's no trouble." Michael put his card down. "Give him a nice view."

Fola laughed properly, which is rarer at a hotel desk than people think, and then spent four minutes doing something complicated with two reservations and a rate code, and Michael leaned on the desk and watched the traffic of the place: a man going past with a chess clock in each hand; two women in fern arguing amiably about a slide; a great shuffling release of banking delegates out of Hall 2 at a quarter past five.

Nobody looked at him. He noticed it and did not, at the time, give it a name. He had walked into buildings for a dozen years and been the most senior person in them, and here he was a man in a good coat at a desk.

"You're on the fourth, Mr Halloway."

"Michael."

"Michael. And you're speaking, it says here. Closing keynote."

"I am. Two o'clock Monday, which is the slot they give you when they still want you at the conference but they've stopped being frightened of you."

Fola grinned. "I'll come."

"You will not."

"I might."

"Fola," said Michael, taking his key card, "nobody has ever come."

---

At 5:22 the four of them were in the lobby at the same time. This was noticed by nobody, and is recorded here only because it happened.

Nik was at the noticeboard reading the round-one pairings, which had just gone up: white on board thirty-one. Behind him a woman in fern was saying "—but the Kettering number *is* the number—" into a phone.

Kate was at the lift, four metres away, thumb moving, bag sliding off her shoulder. She hitched it up without looking.

Alex came out of the second lift as she got into the first. He had come down because there was no signal on nine and had discovered there was no signal in the lobby either, and he stood in the middle of the floor holding his phone up at an angle, turning slowly, a posture with no dignity available to it.

And Michael, walking away from the desk, stopped, because his shoelace had come undone, and set down his case, and knelt to tie it.

Kneeling took him a second longer than it used to. Getting up, he put a hand on the case.

Then he took a breath in, and let it out, and went to find the lift.
