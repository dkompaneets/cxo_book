# 27. Friday

Nik got there first, at ten to six, because he had left Guildford absurdly early and had walked around Liverpool Street for forty minutes in the cold rather than be late.

The pub was called The Fen, on a corner off Sun Street, and it was old in the real way rather than the decorated way: dark wood gone darker, a fire that actually worked, a floor that sloped. Nik took a table by the fire, a table for four, and hung his coat over one of the chairs, and sat down, and did not look at the menu, and waited.

He had brought, in his coat pocket, for no reason he could have defended, a small wooden chess piece — a white king, from the travel set, worn pale on one side by a previous owner. He took it out and stood it on the table and looked at it and put it back in his pocket.

Alex came in at four minutes past six.

He came in the way he came into everywhere, fast, already apologising, scanning the room with the headphones round his neck — and then he saw Nik, and stopped, and his whole face changed, and neither of them managed to do the thing that men do, the nod, the *all right*, the cool version. Nik stood up too fast and knocked the table and Alex said "*mate*" in a voice that had nothing guarded in it at all, and they did not shake hands, they did the other thing, the brief hard clasp with a hand on the shoulder, and then both sat down slightly embarrassed and enormously pleased.

"You look—" said Nik.

"Yeah, you too. You look — did you win?"

"I lost the last one. On purpose, sort of. I'll tell you." Nik was grinning. "I came second overall. Ninety pounds."

"Ninety *pounds*."

"It's not a business," said Nik, and Alex laughed, and it was, for an hour, exactly the way it had been on the roof.

---

They talked over each other. That was the thing. They had an hour before Kate came, and they spent it the way you spend it with someone you were honest with once, under strange conditions, and have not seen since: quickly, greedily, catching up on facts as a way of not yet saying the other thing.

Alex told him about Ben, about the meeting with no laptops, about *once, slowly*, and Nik put his hand flat on the table and said, "That's my — Alex, that's the thing your dad said, I've been using that, I've been using it for four months, ask Meera," and Alex said, "I know. I gave it to you. You gave it back," and they both sat with that for a second.

Nik told him about the memo, and the number he'd said out loud in his own kitchen, and the sambar with too much water, and Rohan pointing at the chair. He told him about Wilf, and *what is it you think you're protecting*, and the empty room behind the door. Alex said, "Did you work it out? What it was?" and Nik said, "The four seconds. When they haven't got it yet and they're deciding about you," and Alex went completely still and said, "Oh. Yeah. That's — yeah. That's mine too. That's exactly mine," and the two of them, the CFO and the CTO, sat in a pub off Sun Street having independently arrived, from opposite directions, at the same four seconds.

"How's your dad?" said Nik.

"Good. His knee — no, that's yours. His shoulder's the same. He's good. We talk properly now." Alex turned his glass. "He said a thing about — he said the sacrifice isn't a debt. That I can't pay it back because there's nobody to pay, and it's not a loan, it's an inheritance, and you don't pay back an inheritance, you spend it." He said it carefully, getting it right. "I think about it every day."

"That's a good father," said Nik.

"Yeah," said Alex. "He is." And then, because he could, because it was that kind of hour: "So's the one you're being. For the record. The chair thing. Rohan pointing at the chair. You know that's — you did that. You're doing that."

Nik looked at the fire for a moment.

"Where's Michael?" he said. "He's usually the one holding court by now. He'll come in and there'll be a thing with the barman and we'll all end up—"

And the door opened, and it was Kate.

---

She came in out of the rain in a dark coat, and she saw them, and she came over, and she did not sit down.

They both knew. That was the thing Kate saw as she crossed the room, and it was almost the worst part: that they both stood up, smiling, ready to be glad, Nik already saying "Kate—", and she watched the smiles begin to go as she got closer, because whatever was in her face was arriving ahead of her.

"Sit down," she said. "Please. Sit down a second."

They sat.

She stayed standing, with her hands on the back of the empty chair — the fourth chair, the one Nik had put Michael's non-existent coat over an hour ago and then quietly taken back — and she said it the way Michael had taught her to say the thing they don't want to hear: plainly, once, without making them wait for it, without a preamble that would let them start hoping in the gap.

"Michael died," she said. "Nine days ago. I rang him on Thursday to ask him here, and his daughter answered, and — he'd been ill for over a year. A heart thing that turned into something else. He knew. He'd known the whole time. He didn't tell anyone."

Nobody said anything.

The fire did its thing. Somewhere behind them a man laughed at a different table, in a different world.

"He was going to come tonight," said Kate. Her voice was very level, and it was the same level as the voicemail, the surgical calm, except that this time she was using it on purpose, to get the words out without them breaking in her mouth. "He said yes. Before he — his daughter said he'd talked about the hotel, at the end. About all of us. The boy with the marker pen. The man who plays chess." She looked at each of them. "The woman who gave him a job that was a telephone."

She sat down, finally, in her own chair, and put her hands flat on the table, and Nik saw that they were not quite steady, and that she had let him see it.

"He said it was the first thing in years that had needed him and not the job," Kate said. "That's what she told me. So."

---

They sat with it.

Alex was looking at the table. His jaw was working. He had, everyone at the table could see, gone somewhere.

"I never called him back," he said.

"Alex—"

"He left me a voicemail. In September. He rang and I didn't pick up and he left a voicemail and I've listened to it three times and I never — I kept meaning to do it at the weekend. I was still angry. I said a thing to him, the last night, I called him a mascot, I said — and he rang me *in September* to say I'd been right, and to say sorry about the introduction, and I thought, I'll call him back when I'm less — " He stopped. "I did the thing my dad warned me about. The debt that gets bigger the longer you leave it. I did it to Michael."

Nobody told him it was all right, because they were not, any of them, in the business of that any more.

Then Alex took out his phone.

"It's forty seconds," he said. "Do you want to—"

"Yes," said Nik.

"Yes," said Kate.

Alex put the phone in the middle of the table, on the dark wood, between the four glasses, and turned it up, and pressed play.

---

*Alex. It's Michael. From the — from the hotel. The mascot.*

His voice came out of the small speaker into the pub, and it was completely, unbearably him: the warmth, the timing, the tiny pause before the joke that you could hear him choosing not to fully make.

*Listen. You were right about most of it, and I've thought about it a great deal, and I wanted to say so, and I wanted to say that the introduction — I should have sent it to you. Just you. You were right about that, and I knew the reason while I was doing it, and you named it, and it's cost me some sleep, which at my age is a genuine sacrifice.*

Kate had her hand over her mouth.

They had braced for last words. These were not last words. Whatever he had known in September, he had not known how little was left of it, and so there was nothing in the recording that was saying goodbye — only a man on an ordinary Tuesday ringing somebody up and putting the true thing first, without going round by the joke.

*Anyway. I hear the recon thing worked. Of course it did. Ring me back if you want to. And if you don't, that's — I'd understand that too.*

A breath. You could hear him deciding.

And then, at the very end, because he could not help it:

*Right. That's the message. I'd say don't let it go to your head, but I've met your head, so — too late. Goodbye.*

You could hear him smiling. Pleased with the small joke. In a room in Hertfordshire, in September, alive.

The message stopped.

Nobody at the table said anything.

---

There was a long time where nobody said anything.

The fire ticked. The phone screen went dark on the wood between them. At the bar someone ordered a round and it was all, distantly, going on, the whole ordinary Friday of it.

Then Nik got up, without a word, and went to the bar, and came back with four glasses, four, on a tray, and set them down around the table — one in front of Kate, one in front of Alex, one in front of himself.

And one in front of the empty chair.

He poured — it was a bottle of red, the second-cheapest, though he did not know that Kate had noticed and that it had undone her a little — and he filled all four, including the fourth, right up, and set the bottle down, and sat.

The fourth glass stood there in front of the empty chair, full, dark, catching the light off the fire.

Nobody moved it. Nobody said *to Michael*, or *he'd have wanted*, or any of the sentences that exist to make a moment easier.

They just left it where it was.

Alex, after a while, picked up his own glass and held it, not raised, just held, in both hands, the way you hold something to warm your hands on it.

"He put a joke on the end," he said. "For me. So I wouldn't—" He stopped. "He did it for me."

"Yes," said Kate.

"He did it every time," said Nik. "That's what it was for."

"Yes," said Kate.

And they sat there, the three of them, with the fourth glass full in front of the empty chair, and the rain on the window, and the fire, and after a long while Kate said, quietly, not as a toast, just as a fact she wanted said out loud in a room with a face in it:

"Same time next Friday."

"Yes," said Nik.

"Yeah," said Alex.

Somebody paid for the fourth glass — it was Nik, at the end, quietly, when he settled the tab, and nobody argued about it, and nobody drank it, and the pub cleared it away after they had gone and thought nothing of it, the way a hotel resets a lobby, the way the world routes around the thing that is no longer there.

And the three of them came back the next Friday, and the one after that.
