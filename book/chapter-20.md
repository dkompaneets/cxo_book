# 20. Everything Said

Nik got there first, at five to nine, because he had been sitting in his room since half past seven unable to be in it. He took the round table in the corner of The Long Room, ordered a lime and soda, looked at it, and ordered a beer as well. He had not replied to Meera. That fact had been underneath everything for twenty-six hours: nine things typed and none sent, then, that morning, *I'll sort it, don't worry, I'll call you tonight,* and back, a single word, *ok,* which had sat in his phone all day like a stone in a shoe.

Alex came in at four minutes past nine, fast, jacket still on, and sat without taking it off. In his pocket was a phone with `#bd-pipeline` on it and a message from Ben at 6:12: *Tomorrow's going to be big. Enjoy the family time mate, you've earned it 🙏*

Michael arrived at 9:11 from the far end of the bar, where he had been since twenty past six, carrying a fresh drink and a face that would have got him through a board meeting. Kate came in at 9:20, twenty minutes late, off a call, sliding the phone into her bag as she sat.

And for eleven minutes it was fine.

That was the thing all three of them would come back to, differently, over the months: that it was *fine*. Michael did a routine about the conference's closing lunch. Alex told the Alabama story and Nik laughed and the coat came into it. Kate said "the roof" and everyone spoke at once. Then Michael said, "Right — it's the last night, and we've none of us said one true thing since Friday, and I propose we keep it that way," and everyone laughed, and Kate leaned back and said, "Oh, I don't know. I'd quite like to."

---

She had gone thirty hours in the presence of a thing she was forbidden to fix, and she had not, in four years, gone thirty hours without fixing something. So the machine — with nowhere to go and enormous pressure behind it — found three problems it could solve.

"Alex. Your co-founder's going to sell the company. Eleven months, tops, and he'll be lovely about it the whole way, and you'll find out from a document. The tell isn't that he talks over you. The tell is the twenty-two seconds — nobody waits twenty-two seconds unless they've already decided and they need it on the record that they asked. You built something in a hotel this weekend for *free*. What did you charge?"

"I don't—"

"Nik." She turned. "You told me at breakfast what your job's like the way people describe weather. If you can't say a number to your own board — and I've watched you not say one to *me*, twice, and I don't even work with you—"

"Kate," said Michael.

"—then you're not a CFO, you're a very expensive narrator, and someone finds out in a quarter that hurts. And Michael, you should retire, because you've told me yourself you're respected and not needed and it's four o'clock and you don't know what to do with the afternoon, so *go*, before they teach the whole company to plan around you and then one day stop—"

"You're doing the thing," said Alex.

"—I'm being useful."

"You're doing the thing you do. You're solving us so you don't have to sit here." Alex's voice had gone somewhere odd, not loud. "You've diagnosed three people in ninety seconds and you're *pleased.*"

"That's not—"

"It's a bit contemptuous, actually," said Alex, and Kate stopped, because it was, and she knew it was, and there was nowhere in her to put it.

---

It could have ended there. It didn't, because Alex had been sitting on the CC thread for twenty-nine hours and Kate had just said *you built something this weekend for free*, and it came out of him sideways and at the wrong person.

"You made an introduction," he said, to Michael.

Michael put his glass down. "I did."

"At three in the morning. And by nine the next day my co-founder had it, and by half nine he'd booked a meeting for tomorrow that I'm not in, and by two he'd turned it into a compliance sale — the thing I said in June was a mannequin — and the reason I'm not in the room is *you*. You handed him the only thing I've ever built that anyone outside a computer has valued. You put my name in the email — *talk to him, not about him,* I read it — and it took him eleven hours to route around me with it."

"Alex, I said I'd write. You said two o'clock Monday—"

"Why didn't you send it to *me*?"

Michael did not answer, and the not-answering was the answer, and Alex saw it.

"You didn't do it for me," he said. "You did it because you liked being the man who makes an introduction at three in the morning. No committee, no phone ringing, so you found a kid with a marker pen and got to be somebody's benefactor for four hours, and then you sent it to the CEO instead of the engineer, because that's who men like you send things to. You're a mascot. You're the funny old man everyone's fond of who hasn't said one real thing all weekend, and you'd rather be liked than useful, and I know exactly what it is because I do the same thing with silence—"

"That's enough," said Kate.

"No, it isn't," said Alex, and stood up, and then didn't have anywhere to go, and sat down again, which was worse.

---

"Right," said Nik, into it, too quickly, half up, patting his jacket. "I'm getting these. My shout — I've had a good week, four out of six, and it's been a good year, we came off a strong quarter, so this one's on the studio—"

"Lodestone Games," said Kate.

Nik stopped, half standing.

"That's who you're at. You said the studio and the Sunward milestone at breakfast and I looked it up, because I look everything up. Your publisher's got a clawback on that milestone at the end of November — it was in a piece in January — two-point-four million, against a runway that's six months if it slips, and it'll slip, because the producer's missed every date they've set. And you've just told this table you're buying a round off a strong quarter, in front of two people who can't check and one who can. Why?"

"Kate," said Michael. "Leave it—"

"Because I have four hundred and eleven pounds," said Nik.

The table went quiet in a different way.

"That's what's in the account," he said, and sat back down, and his voice had gone flat and careful, the accent a shade more there than it had been all weekend. "My daughter's school payment bounced on Friday. I sent my father money on Wednesday out of the wrong account. I've run the family budget four times in that room upstairs and there's no version that works. So — no. It has not been a strong quarter, and I don't know why I said it, except that I always say it, and my wife said *that's nice, Nik,* and I've been thinking about the way she said it for two days."

"Nik, I'm sorry," said Kate. "That was—"

"It's fine."

"It isn't."

"No," he said, "it isn't, but there's nothing else to say, so."

---

Michael had not spoken for four minutes. When he did it was in a voice none of them had heard, because it had nothing in front of it — no warmth, no timing, no joke waiting at the end of the sentence to take the weight off it.

"You're right," he said, to Alex. "I should have sent it to you. I knew the reason while I was doing it and you've said it out loud and I'll not sleep tonight. So — yes. I'm sorry, and I'll fix the part I can fix."

Alex, who had braced against everything except that, said nothing.

"And now I'm going to give you the other half, because you've earned it and because nobody else in your life is ever going to." Michael did not raise his voice. "Nobody talks over you. I've watched you four days and I don't believe it. What happens is that everyone in your company waits — and you don't come, and after a while they get on with it, because a business has to. You've trained forty people to spare you. It isn't cruelty, it's a service they provide, and you've been accepting it for two years and calling it being silenced. The day you stop, it stops. And then you'll find out whether you were right, which is the thing you've actually been avoiding."

"That's—" Alex's mouth was open.

"Yes," said Michael. "It is."

He turned to Nik. "And you didn't lie to your wife to spare her. You've told yourself that for years and it's the wrong way round. You did it because a man who says the plain thing has to sit there afterwards while somebody decides what to make of him, and you'd rather be four hundred pounds from the edge on your own than looked at for four seconds by someone who loves—"

"You don't know my marriage," said Nik, sharp, and Michael stopped, and there was a second where it could have gone either way.

"No," said Michael. "I don't. I take that back. That was mine, not yours." And it was the taking-back — a man conceding a point in the middle of the worst thing he'd said all weekend — that told them how far off the map they'd gone.

He looked at Kate last, and this one he got out whole. "You solved three strangers tonight, brilliantly, because on Sunday you did something you can't fix from a hotel and you'd rather take a room apart than sit with it. And you've asked me to be your advisor, and I'll do it, and you'll ring me for twenty years and take nothing, because you don't want advice. You want a witness who'll tell you you were right to be tired."

Kate did not move.

"There," said Michael. He stood, and put a note under his glass, more than the drinks. "That's everything I've thought all weekend. Most of it's true. None of you asked for it. I'll be ashamed of it in the morning and I'll not take a word of it back except the one I already did."

"Michael—" said Kate.

"Goodnight," said Michael Halloway, and went, and they heard him say something to Denny at the bar on his way past — something ordinary, something with a lift on the end of it — and heard Denny laugh, and that, somehow, was the worst part.

---

They sat there.

Denny came and cleared two glasses and did not ask if they wanted anything, which was the kindest thing anyone did.

"He was right," Nik said eventually.

"About all of it," said Kate.

Alex was staring at the door. "I shouldn't have said mascot."

Nobody told him it was all right. They tried twice more to talk — Kate said something about the roof, Nik said something about the last round in the morning — and both times the thing got up onto its feet and sat back down. At eleven they went up.

It had taken forty minutes, and the part that did the damage was the eleven at the start, when it was fine, and none of them had felt it coming.
