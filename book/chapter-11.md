# 11. The Roof

"There's a roof," said Michael.

It was ten past nine on the Friday evening. Kate had come out of the Warwick Suite at eight-fifty having spent eleven hours being certain in front of sixty-one people, and had walked into the lobby with the specific hollowed-out feeling of a woman who has just been excellent, and Michael had been coming the other way out of Hall 2 with a conference tote bag he had no intention of ever opening.

"There's a what?"

"A roof. There's a staff terrace above the ballroom. It's on the fifth, you go up in the service lift or you go through the door by the ice machine on the fifth which they've never once locked, and there's a flat bit with a rail round it and an extractor and about four chairs, and you can see the whole thing from up there."

"How do you know that?"

"I stayed here in 2004," said Michael, "when it was a different hotel with the same concrete. There was a bloke called Ray who used to smoke up there and he showed me. Nobody's changed the door."

Kate looked at him.

"Is this legal?"

"It is *unspecified*," said Michael.

---

It was not, in any sense that a photograph would support, a nice place.

There was an extractor housing the size of a transit van that made a low continuous roar and, every ninety seconds or so, a click. There was a rubber-membrane floor. There was a rail. There were, as promised, four chairs, three of them plastic and stacked, one of them a proper metal one that somebody had brought up here at some point in the last two decades for reasons of their own. There were eleven cigarette ends in a paint tin, and a broom, and a view.

The view was the car park, and then the ring road, and then the M42, and then — because The Ardenne sat on slightly raised ground, and because it was ten past nine in July — everything else: the whole flat lit sprawl of it going off to the north-west, the tower blocks in the middle distance, the aircraft stacked over the airport in a slow queue with their lights on, and the sky doing the long orange thing it does in England in July at that hour, where it takes forty minutes to give up.

And there was already somebody there.

He was sitting in the metal chair with his elbows on his knees, and he stood up quickly when the door went, and it was the man from breakfast.

"Oh," said Kate.

"Sorry — I'll—"

"No. Don't. Sit down." She was laughing. "This is — I'm sorry, do you have a key to the roof?"

"I found it looking for a fire exit," said Nik. "On Thursday. I've been up twice."

"This is Michael," said Kate. "He also breaks into roofs. Michael, this is—"

"Nik."

"Michael."

The two men shook hands, which on a roof, at night, next to an extractor, is a slightly comic act, and all three of them registered that and nobody said anything about it.

---

Alex came up at twenty to ten, holding his phone above his head.

He came through the door already talking. "—yeah, no, I've got one bar, I'm going to lose you, I'm going to — Ben. *Ben.* I'll do it in the morning." He stopped dead. Three faces. "Oh. Sorry. God. Sorry."

"He's off the chess," said Nik.

"He's off the *what*?"

"He's the one who lost in nineteen moves."

"That's — " Alex pointed. "That is a completely unfair way to introduce someone."

"It's how I'd want to be introduced," said Michael, "if it were true of me, which it will be by Sunday. Sit down. There's a chair, it's plastic, it's from 1998, I make no promises."

And Alex, who had come up here to shout at a phone and who had been intending, forty seconds earlier, to go back to his room and work until two, sat down.

---

Here is what nobody said, over the next hour and a half.

Nobody said what they did. Michael said "banks" once, in passing, in a sentence about the fraud stands in Hall 2. Kate said "e-commerce," in a joke about warehouses. Nik said he was "with a games company," and Alex said "I do security stuff," and that was the entire exchange of professional information among four people whose combined direct reports numbered three hundred and eleven.

Nobody's title came up. Not once. And the extraordinary thing — Kate thought about this afterwards, quite hard — was that it was not tact. It was not four people being modest. It was that on a roof, at night, beside an extractor, with a paint tin full of somebody else's cigarettes, there was no mechanism by which it could have come up, because the subject never turned to what anybody was worth.

What they talked about was: the hotel. The lanyards, at length, and the incident with the lanyards, which Michael had got the full account of from Fola and told very badly and very well. The fireplace with no fire in it. Whether the nine hundred glass rods in the lobby were, as Alex insisted, structurally supporting anything, and the fact that Nik knew — actually knew, from a man in the analysis room — that they were not, and that the man in question was an eighty-three-year-old structural engineer who had walked into the lobby on Thursday, looked up at them for four seconds, and said the word "decoration" with genuine contempt.

They talked about Birmingham. They talked about a hotel in Frankfurt Nik had once been stuck in for three days. Michael did four minutes on a conference in 1991 that had gone so wrong that a man had ended up in a fountain, and there was a point in it where Kate had to put her drink down.

And below them, coming up through the roof, very faintly, under the extractor, there was music.

The Bellwether summit had ended its second day with a thing in the ballroom, and the thing in the ballroom had a DJ, and what came through five inches of concrete and a rubber membrane was not the tune but the low end of it — the bass and the kick, arriving in the soles of the feet rather than the ears.

---

The bass came up through the membrane, and Kate, sitting on the low wall with a glass in her hand and Michael halfway through a story about a man from Basildon, put her right foot flat and — without any awareness of it at all — began to keep time. Not tapping. Keeping: the heel down, the ball of the foot taking the beat, and then, at some point, the shoulder coming into it, a centimetre, on the two and the four. She had done it in her sleep since she was six. She had done it in a church hall in Amersham in 1993.

Then her phone lit her lap, face up, a message from Tom about the Marrowbone file, and her hand went to it out of thirty years of reflex, and the foot stopped, and she read the message and thumbed a reply and the moment closed over as though it had never been open. The other three did not notice it happen. Neither did she.

The rest of them let go more easily, being further from the office than she was.

Nik, with no requirement to be in the conversation, set up a position in his head — round two that afternoon, the fifteen-year-old from Coventry who'd played the opening better than he had and then made the mistake a strong fifteen-year-old makes, which is to be certain. He replayed it from move twenty, giving her the better line, and found it was still fine for him, and gave her a better line again, and took no part in the talk for what must have been ten minutes while making, somewhere in the middle of it, two remarks that everyone found funny and that he could not afterwards recall.

And Alex, during a gap when the other two were arguing about whether the M42 was worse than the M25, took the notebook out of his jacket — the first time in eighteen days — and turned past the last entry and wrote, in the small square handwriting, in the dark, badly: *syscall audit — batch on the ring buffer, flush on quiesce not interval. also ask him about the second-question thing.* Then he sat with it open on his knee for a while, and put it away.

And Michael went to the far end of the roof, past the extractor, because his phone was buzzing, and rang home.

It was twenty to eleven, which was too late, and Claire picked up because she was up anyway.

"Dad? Is everything all right?"

"Everything's fine. Is he awake?"

"He's — Dad, it's twenty to eleven."

"I know. It's just there's — no, it's nothing. I'll ring tomorrow."

There was a rustle, and then Claire's voice further off, and then, very close and very loud, a six-year-old:

"HELLO?"

"Sam."

"Grandad, I'm not asleep."

"I can hear that."

"I'm not asleep because Iris is doing the *piano*."

"Is she any good?"

There was a pause of great seriousness.

"No," said Sam.

Michael laughed out loud, standing on a roof in Birmingham next to an extractor, and then he said, "Where are you? Are you in bed?" and Sam said he was under the bed, and Michael said why, and Sam said because of a plan.

And then, for eleven minutes, Michael Halloway had a conversation.

He did not ask how school was. He did not ask what Sam had been doing, which is a question that means *give me a report*, and he did not say *and are you being good for Mummy*, which is a question that means *I have run out*. He asked about the plan. He asked what was under the bed. He was told, at length, about a shoe, and about the fact that Iris said the shoe was hers and it was not hers, it was found. He asked how you could tell a found shoe from an owned shoe and was given a criterion, and objected to the criterion, and was overruled.

At the end of it Claire came back on and said, "What was that?"

"What was what?"

"Nothing," said Claire, in a strange voice. "He's very pleased with himself. Night, Dad."

Michael put the phone in his pocket and stood at the rail for a moment looking at the lights of the aircraft going round.

Then he took a breath in, and let it out, and went back to the others.

---

At ten past midnight they came down.

It was Kate who said it, at the door by the ice machine, with the fluorescent corridor light making all four of them look considerably worse than they had on the roof.

"Right," she said. "Last night. Monday. All of us, down there, that bar, whatever time. Actual drink, not a — you know. Not a networking."

"Yes," said Nik, immediately.

"I'm speaking at two on Monday," said Michael, "so I'll either be celebrating or drinking, and both of those want company."

"I'll be here," said Alex. "I mean, I'll be — yeah. I'll be here."

And all four of them meant it, entirely, in the way strangers do: without a calendar invitation, without a follow-up, without any of the machinery by which grown people usually make a plan, and therefore — as each of them understood perfectly well while agreeing to it — with a much higher probability of it actually happening.
