# 9. Nineteen Moves

At twenty past eleven the congress had finished for the night and the atrium had reverted to being an atrium, but four boards were still out at the far end, where a handful of people were playing blitz for nothing.

Alex had come down at eleven because the wifi on the ninth floor had lost a build halfway through and he had needed to put twenty metres between himself and a laptop before he did something regrettable to it. He walked the length of the atrium twice. He got a coffee out of a machine that produced a substance.

Then he stopped at the boards.

There was one man sitting alone at the end table with a position set up, not playing anyone, moving pieces about with one hand and writing on a sheet with the other. Fortyish. Reading glasses. A tournament badge on a fern-adjacent lanyard that was, on inspection, a fourth colour altogether.

Alex watched him for a minute.

Alex had played a good deal of chess between the ages of nine and fifteen, in the way that a certain sort of boy does: a school club, a couple of county things, one weekend in Hastings where he had come eleventh. He had stopped when he found compilers. He still played online at three in the morning sometimes, badly and at speed, in a way he would not have described to anybody as playing chess.

He was rated, in his own estimation, *decent*.

"Are you allowed to play people," he said, "or is it all official?"

The man looked up.

"It's a hotel," he said. "You're allowed to do anything."

"Do you want a game?"

There was the smallest pause.

"All right," said Nik.

---

It took nineteen moves.

Alex could reconstruct the first eight afterwards and never got much further than that. He played e5, which he had always played. He developed his knights, which is correct. On move six he pushed a pawn to gain a tempo, which felt strong.

On move nine he was fine. He was still, at move nine, telling himself a story in which he was fine, and in which the man opposite was a hobbyist in a hotel with a badge on a lanyard, and in which Alex Wójcik, who reasoned about lock-free data structures for a living, was about to have quite an enjoyable half hour.

On move twelve his position stopped having any moves in it.

That was the sensation, and it was not like losing. Losing he knew. This was something else: he looked at the board and there were, in the technical sense, about thirty legal moves available, and every single one of them was worse than the last one, and there was no plan, and there had not been a plan since roughly move seven, and the man opposite had not done anything he could point at.

"Sorry," Alex said, "what happened?"

"On move six," said Nik, "you took a pawn's worth of space, and after that all your pieces had to go behind it."

"Right."

"It's a very common thing."

On move seventeen Nik put a knight on a square from which it attacked nothing at all, and Alex looked at it for two minutes and then understood that it attacked nothing at all *yet*, and made a small involuntary noise.

He resigned on nineteen.

"That was—" He rubbed his face. "That was actually humiliating. Thank you."

"You play quickly," said Nik. "You're not bad. You just play like it's a race."

"It's the internet's fault."

"It's always the internet's fault." Nik was already resetting, pieces going back with the fast economical hand of a man who has done it forty thousand times. "Again?"

"Can we do one where I last past move nine?"

"We can try."

---

The second game took an hour and a half and neither of them was really playing it.

What happened instead was the thing that happens over a chessboard between two strangers and almost nowhere else, which is that talking becomes possible precisely because talking is not the point. There is somewhere to look. There are gaps of two or three minutes where nobody has to say anything, and the gaps are not awkward, because the gaps are load-bearing.

Alex found out that the man was called Nik, that he was a CFO, that he had two children, that the younger one had been up at one o'clock every night for eight months, and that he had come here for five days and had spent nine of the first eleven hours playing chess.

Nik found out that the boy — he thought of him as the boy, and would have been embarrassed by that — was twenty-six, and had a startup, and that when he said the word *interceptor* his hands started moving.

"So what does it actually do," Nik said.

"You'll be bored."

"I've just spent two hours on move seventeen of a game I won. I have an infinite capacity for boredom."

So Alex told him, and did the thing with his hands, and used the words *ring buffer* and *below the runtime* and stopped twice to say "sorry, this is—" and Nik said "no, keep going," and meant it. And at some point during it Alex realised he had spoken without interruption for about four minutes, and that the reason was not that Nik was being polite but that Nik was moving a bishop about and thinking, and had no particular need to say anything.

Then, moving the bishop, Nik said:

"How is it, having a co-founder?"

And Alex said, without any preparation at all: "He talks over me."

---

It got to about one in the morning.

They had stopped even pretending about the second game around midnight; the position sat there between them like a table decoration.

"The thing that gets me," Alex said, "is that he's not — he's genuinely a good person. That's what makes it impossible. If he was a shark I could deal with a shark. Every single time it happens, if you filmed it and showed it to a jury, he's done nothing. He asks what I think. He waits. Once, in March, he sat there for a full — I timed it — twenty-two seconds."

"And you didn't say it."

"I said about a third of it. In a voice." Alex looked at the board. "You know when you can hear yourself? Like from outside? My voice goes — I can hear it dropping while it's happening, and I can't get hold of it. It's not that I'm scared of him. It's not fear, it's more like — the words just aren't there. It's like reaching into a pocket."

Nik sat very still.

"Yes," he said.

"You get that?"

"I got that for about five years," said Nik. "Different reason."

And then he said it.

He had not decided to. He was aware of having not decided to, in the same way you are aware of a step that isn't there.

"When I came here I had an accent that people had to work at," he said. "Not a bad one. But there'd be a — half a second, and they'd lean in, and say 'sorry?' And the sentence would go into the room four seconds late and about ten per cent smaller than everyone else's. And that happened maybe forty times a week for five years." He turned a captured pawn over in his fingers. "So I did what you do. I stopped talking. Which cost me a promotion in 2005 to a man who was worse than me, and I knew exactly why, and it was the correct decision by the company, because he'd said four thousand things and I'd said sixty."

"So then what?"

"So then I found the other thing," said Nik.

He put the pawn down.

"I polish," he said.

Alex looked up.

"I don't lie," said Nik. "I want to be very clear, because I've thought about this a great deal and it matters to me. I have never put a false number in front of a board in my life. It's all in the pack. It's all true. But if there's a bad thing on page nineteen and a good thing on page four, I know exactly which one I'm putting on page four, and I know exactly what a room does when I say 'healthy' instead of 'adequate'. And I do it every single time. I did it eleven days ago. My chair asked me a direct question about a clawback that could take our runway from fourteen months to six, and I said I was *relaxed*." He said the word with a delicacy that made it sound like something he had picked up off the floor. "And afterwards the CEO rang me and said I make it feel like there's a floor under everything."

The air handling hummed. Two tables away a man was putting a set into a bag.

"Why?" said Alex.

"Because if I say the plain thing they might get it wrong," said Nik. "That's it. That's the whole of it, and it sounds insane out loud, which is presumably why I've never said it out loud." He looked at the board. "I have said, to a stranger, at one in the morning, in a hotel, a thing I have not said to my wife in fourteen years of marriage. That's — I'd like it noted that I'm aware of how that looks."

"Noted," said Alex.

"Right."

"It's fine, though," said Alex. "I don't count. You'll never see me again."

"No," Nik agreed. "That's the entire mechanism."

---

At ten past one Alex said, "My parents get here Saturday."

"Where from?"

"London. They flew in from — no, they live in London, they've lived in London for thirty years. My dad came over in ninety-four." He turned his king over and stood it back up. "They booked a hotel in Alabama. Not on purpose."

Nik laughed, and it was an entirely different laugh from the one he had used four times in the last hour.

"You'll like him," said Alex, and then heard himself, and added, "sorry, that's — I don't know why I said that."

"I might be here."

"Yeah." Alex stood up. "Anyway. Nineteen moves."

"Nineteen moves."

"Are you playing tomorrow?"

"Two o'clock. And every day after that until Tuesday."

"Right." Alex hovered. He was extremely bad at the end of things; there was always a beat where he did not know what the shape was. "Well — good luck. With the — with the guarding thing."

Nik looked up sharply.

"I didn't say a guarding thing."

"You said you defend positions that don't need defending. You said it about twenty minutes ago, about the game."

"Did I."

"Yeah."

"Ah," said Nik, and sat back, and put his hand flat on the table beside the board.

Alex went up in the lift to the ninth floor. He got into bed at half past one with his laptop on his knees and did not open it. He lay there for a while.

Then he reached over to the chair, and got the jacket, and took the notebook out of the inside pocket, and held it on his chest without opening it, and fell asleep like that with the light on.
