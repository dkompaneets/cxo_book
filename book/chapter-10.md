# 10. Something That Isn't a Race

The breakfast room at The Ardenne had been designed for a hotel running at sixty per cent, and on the Friday it was running at ninety-four, and the result was a scene from a documentary about a lifeboat.

Four hundred banking delegates had come down between half seven and eight because the first session was at half eight. Sixty-one Bellwether people had been told the summit resumed at nine. A hundred and seventy-six chess players were mostly still asleep, apart from a dozen English juniors of about fourteen who could each eat their own body weight in bacon.

Kate stood at the entrance with a plate in one hand and her phone in the other and performed the calculation everyone in the room had performed, which was that there were no tables. Except one, at the far end by the window, with a man at it and three empty chairs.

"Is anyone—"

"No," said Nik, moving a folder. "Please."

---

He had eggs and beans and a book face down beside the plate, and she had read the spine of it upside down before she sat, because she read everything.

"Is that chess?"

"It is."

"Are you the chess?" She gestured with her fork toward the atrium. "There is an *enormous* amount of chess happening in this building."

"A hundred and seventy-six of us."

"Is that a lot?"

"For a Midlands weekend congress it's about right." He said it without any performance at all, which she noticed. "The British is fifteen hundred."

She ate. She had not sat opposite a stranger at breakfast in perhaps six years; her breakfasts happened standing up, or in cars, or in rooms where everyone already had a view of her.

"So do you win money?"

"There's a prize fund. It's a hundred and eighty for first in my section, the entry's forty-eight, and the hotel's a hundred and forty-one a night."

"So you lose."

"Everyone loses. It's not a business." He drank some tea. "The man who won it two years ago drove up from Bristol and slept in the car."

Kate laughed, and then her mind did the only thing it knew how to do, which was to go looking for the mechanism. "So what *is* it? Presumably at some level it's about winning, or you'd play at home."

Nik put his cup down.

"Do you want the answer I give at work," he said, "or the actual one?"

"Sorry — is that a real distinction?"

"Yes."

"Then the actual one. Obviously."

---

"It's the quiet," said Nik.

He said it and stopped, and looked out at the car park, and Kate — whose instinct in any silence was to fill it — sat with her fork in her hand and, to her own surprise, waited.

"There's a moment about forty minutes into a game. Everyone's settled, the openings are done, the arbiter's stopped walking about. You know when you take earplugs out and hear how loud it had been?" He turned his cup a little. "It's that, in reverse. There are a hundred and seventy-six people in a room and not one of them is going to speak for three hours. Nobody's going to ask me anything. Nobody's going to be told something about me and then look at me." He hunted for a word, didn't find it, and took the ordinary one. "There's no explaining."

"Right," said Kate.

"And the position is completely true. It doesn't matter how I present it. There's no page four and no page nineteen." A short laugh at something she had no context for. "If I'm winning I'm winning, and if I'm lost I'm lost, and nobody has ever gone home from a chess tournament having got away with it."

Kate had stopped eating.

"Is that restful," she said, "or is it terrifying?"

"Restful. God. Restful." He seemed slightly startled at himself, and reached for the toast. "There's nothing to *hold.*"

---

She very nearly told him.

It came up in her so fast and so completely that she actually opened her mouth to do it. *I used to dance.* The whole thing was there, assembled, ready — the church hall in Amersham, the hairspray, Tuesdays and Saturdays, the eleven-plus tutor, the five weeks in March that nobody mentioned.

And in the half-second before it left her, some piece of machinery she did not know she owned reviewed it and found the flaw. The story ended thirty years ago. If she told it, this man would look at her with the particular sympathy people reserve for a thing that is over, and then she would have to say something, and the something would be *yes, well*, and ninety seconds down that road she would either have to make a joke or say out loud, to a stranger, in a breakfast room, *I stopped when I was eleven and I have never once gone back and I am forty-one.*

"That's nice," she said. "That you've got something that isn't a race."

"You don't?"

"I've got — I mean, I run. I've got a thing on the wall that tells me how I ran." She heard exactly how it sounded and went on anyway, at speed, in the warm voice, the good one. "I've got a Peloton I use as a coat rack, which I maintain is the correct use, and I once went to a spa where a woman told me my shoulders were the worst she'd seen that year, and I took it as a competitive result."

He laughed. It worked. It always worked; she had built it to work.

And he did not push, because there was nothing visible to push at — as far as Nik Raghavan could see, this was a funny, quick, formidable woman in a fern lanyard who ran.

Kate poured more coffee and was, for a moment, very slightly frightened by how easy that had been.

---

The room emptied at twenty past eight the way rooms do, all at once.

"I should go," she said. "Sixty-one people and a whiteboard."

"Doing what?"

"Deciding a number." She stacked her cup on her plate, which was a thing her mother did. "Can I ask you something, since you're a CFO and you're not mine? When somebody tells a board a thing they don't want to hear and it goes badly — what actually made it go badly? Not the theory."

Nik thought about it properly, which took a while.

"Being unable to answer the second question," he said. "You can tell them anything. Anything at all. But you have to have already been where they're going next, and if you haven't, they stop listening to the answer and start looking at you."

"That's very good."

"It's not mine. Someone told me that in 2009 and I've dined out on it ever since."

She stood and gathered her folder, and it was then, entirely offhand, in the middle of putting the chess book in his bag, that Nik said:

"Somebody asked me a question on Thursday night. I've been carrying it round the building ever since."

"What was it?"

"I was playing like a man protecting something. I won, but. And an old boy in the analysis room said—" he straightened up with the bag "—*what is it you think you're protecting?*"

Two seconds.

"Ha," said Kate.

"Yes."

"And what is it?"

"Nothing," said Nik. "That's the annoying part. I went and had a proper look. There's nothing behind the door." He put the bag on his shoulder. "Good luck with your number."

"Good luck with round two."

"Round two's at two, against a fifteen-year-old from Coventry who beat a grandmaster's son in April, so I'd say the pressure is distributed asymmetrically."

He went. Kate watched him cross the wreckage of the breakfast room — an unremarkable man of about forty in an unremarkable jumper — and then picked up her phone, and there were nine things on it, and she dealt with four in the lift.

On eleven she put the folder on the desk and stood a moment at the window with the car park below and the motorway doing its thing in the distance, and found she was not thinking about the twenty-two per cent.

*What is it you think you're protecting.*

And the answer came back so fast, and was so obviously and completely wrong, that she said it out loud to an empty hotel room:

"The company."

Then she picked up the folder and went down to the Warwick Suite and was, for nine hours, magnificent.
