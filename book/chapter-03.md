# 3. Deep Breath

Lauriston had been founded in 1786 by two brothers who fell out in 1791, and the bank had been managing disagreement quietly ever since. Its headquarters was a 1960s building faced in Portland stone that had gone the colour of a filing cabinet, and on the fourth floor, in an office that had once belonged to a man with a knighthood, Michael Halloway approved things.

At 8:14 he approved the revised statement of work for the branch-estate programme, nineteen pages, of which he read the schedule of rates and the termination clause and nothing else, because after thirty-one years he knew that a statement of work is a poem about money and only two of its stanzas are load-bearing.

He signed it. He watched the little animation of the signature going on.

Then he sat back, and took a long breath in through his nose, and let it out.

At 8:19 he took a call from Group Risk about a supplier in Leeds, and said the sentence he had said perhaps two hundred times in his career — *we're not going to solve this in this call, so let's agree what would make us able to solve it in the next one* — and heard the relief come down the line, and put the phone down.

He breathed in. He breathed out.

At 8:36 he declined a request to sit on a steering group, and then, four minutes later, having thought about who had asked and what it would cost her to be declined, un-declined it and accepted, which is a different and worse thing than saying yes in the first place.

Breathed in. Breathed out.

By ten past nine he had done nine things. Nobody had ever counted the breaths, including Michael, who had been taking them since roughly 2011 and did not know he was doing it, in the way that a man does not know the sound of his own house.

---

The programme review was at ten, in Cathedral, the big room, with the screen that never worked first time.

There were nineteen people in it, which was nine too many, and Michael had said so, and had been told that this was a matter of "inclusion in the change journey," which was the kind of phrase that had entered the bank around 2018 like Japanese knotweed. So: nineteen. He sat halfway down the long side, because sitting at the head made people perform, and made a joke about the screen while Callum Deitch fought it, and got a laugh, and the screen came on.

Callum Deitch was thirty-four and extremely good. Michael wished, with an old man's specific and unrepeatable envy, that he were less good, so that this would be easier.

"So, just to level-set for everyone," Callum said, "PROGRAMME ATLAS is the migration of the core deposit ledger off the mainframe estate. We're in wave two of five. Wave two is retail current accounts — six-point-four million accounts — and the cutover window is the August bank holiday, which gives us seventy-two hours."

*Seventy-one*, thought Michael. *You lose an hour to the reconciliation freeze at the front and you never get it back, and in 2019 it was seventy-one and everybody said seventy-two then as well.*

"Wave one delivered in March, slightly behind plan—"

*Nine weeks behind plan.*

"—and the critical path runs through the settlement interface, which is the piece that talks to the payments gateway."

*The Ledger Gap*, thought Michael. *We have called it the Ledger Gap since 2014. Three firms. Nine million pounds. It is still there.*

"The three amber items are data quality in the dormant-account population, vendor resourcing for the September window, and the interface spec sign-off, which is — Michael, actually, that one's with you."

Nineteen faces turned, politely, at the speed of a wave in a stadium.

"It's on my desk," Michael said. "It'll be back to you Friday."

"Brilliant. So the RAG status overall we've kept at amber-green—"

Michael had built the settlement interface. Not the code; but in 2013 he had sat in a room like this one with four people and a whiteboard and drawn the shape of the thing the bank had used ever since, and had argued for it against a Group CIO who wanted to buy something instead, and had won, and had been right, and a decade later the retail bank still ran on the drawing. He knew the dormant-account problem down to the year the data went bad. He knew the vendor resourcing was not amber, it was red, because he knew what that vendor's Manchester office looked like in September when the graduate intake arrived.

He could have said all of it in ninety seconds, and the room would have gone very quiet, and Callum Deitch would have had a bad afternoon.

He let him finish.

He let him finish for two reasons, and the first was the one he would have given if anyone had asked: that a thirty-four-year-old presenting his own programme in front of nineteen people should not be corrected by the COO, because he will present it worse for the rest of his life. That reason was genuine. Michael had believed it for years and had built two careers on it, including Terry Nwosu's.

The second reason was that Michael was tired. Not sleepy — tired in the joints of the thing, tired in the place where the willingness lives. To say it he would have had to gather himself, and to gather himself in front of nineteen people would have shown them what it cost to gather himself, and he would rather have been shot than let that room see how tired he was, and how fed up, and how long it had been since he had last cared what colour the RAG status was.

So he sat, and nodded at the right moments, and at the end said, "Good pack, Callum. Send me the dormant-account numbers, I've got a feeling about the 2009 tranche" — which was, at least, a door left open in a wall.

He waited until the room emptied. Then he sat for a moment at the long table with the screen still glowing behind him, and took a breath in, and let it out, and went to lunch.

---

Lunch was a sandwich at his desk, and the post.

Actual post; Lauriston still had it, in a wire tray, brought round at 12:30 by a man called Errol who had been doing it for nineteen years and had a system for which floors got theirs first that nobody had ever formally approved. A charity thing. A wine merchant. A brochure from a wealth manager who had somehow got hold of his date of birth and had put on the front, in a serif font, *The next chapter deserves a plan* — which Michael read twice, then dropped in the bin from a height, then, after a moment, took out of the bin and put in a drawer.

And the invitation.

Heavy card, embossed, the kind that costs nine pounds a unit and exists so a conference can charge two thousand a head. *The Ardenne, Birmingham. The Banking Operations Forum. We would be honoured if you would deliver the closing keynote.* Forty minutes. Two o'clock on the Monday, after lunch, at the fag-end of a four-day conference — which is a graveyard slot dressed in a dinner jacket, and Michael knew it, and was not sure he minded.

He turned it over. On the back, in biro: *Michael — they asked for you by name. It'd be nice for the bank. F.*

Fiona Redgrave, chief executive, who wrote on the back of things in biro.

Michael read the four words *they asked for you by name* and something happened in his chest that he chose not to look at directly, and which was not entirely pleasure.

He thought about the Monday. The forum ran from the Friday; if he went up early he would be away four days, and there was nothing in those four days he could not move, and the alternative to being at The Ardenne was being in this building, and the two of those, laid side by side, were not close.

He wrote *Delighted — MH* under the biro, and put it in the tray for Errol.

---

In the spring of 1975 a man came to the house in Watford and put a sticker on the television.

Michael was twelve. He remembered the sticker better than he remembered his father's face that year — a printed label about the size of a playing card, gummed onto the top-right corner of the screen where you could see it from the sofa. It stayed there eleven days. Nobody in the house said anything about it. His mother went on wiping around it.

His father had been a partner — one of three — in a builders' merchant on the Dunstable Road. Halloway & Grieves. There had been a van with the name on it, and Michael had once been allowed to ride in the van, standing up, holding the dashboard, which in 1971 was a thing that could happen to a boy.

What Michael did not learn until he was nineteen was that all three partners had signed personal guarantees on the overdraft, which is a sentence that means nothing at all until it means everything. When the property market went over in 1974 and the builders stopped paying and then stopped existing, the firm went down owing forty-one thousand pounds, and the bank — some bank, some quiet, decent, entirely reasonable bank — went to the guarantees.

They lost the house in the November. They moved to a maisonette in Bushey with a smell in the hall.

The thing he carried was not the maisonette. It was two things, and he had never told anyone either of them, including Ruth, in forty years of marriage.

The first was that his father, Ken Halloway, who had been a loud man, a man who sang in the car, stopped talking at meals. Not sulking; Michael had understood at the time that it was not sulking, and had not had the word for what it was, and had it now. It was arithmetic. He was doing arithmetic, at the table, all the way through dinner, every dinner, for two years.

The second was that Michael, aged thirteen, sitting at that table, had made a promise to himself with the total, stupid, unbreakable seriousness of a child, and had then gone and kept it for half a century. The promise had no words at the time. If you made him put words to it now he would say something like: *nobody at my table is ever going to have to do that sum.*

He went into a bank at twenty-two. Not because he liked banks. Because a bank was the largest, dullest, slowest, most survivable thing he could find, and they paid on the same day every month, and there was a pension, and there was no guarantee on earth that anybody could ask him to sign.

Forty-two years. Three institutions. Two mergers survived, one restructure run, six hundred and forty people made redundant by his signature over the years and every one of them, he had made sure, with the outplacement and the extra month. He had never once been out of work. He had never once been overdrawn. He had put two children through school and a daughter through a wedding and had bought a house in 1994 that was now worth an amount of money that embarrassed him, and he had, at sixty-four, precisely the life that a boy in a maisonette in Bushey had set out to build.

He had also, it turned out, missed most of it.

---

*Keynote — draft.*

The cursor had been sitting in that document for nine days.

He had a structure. Getting a structure was easy; he had watched four hundred people present four thousand things and could build a forty-minute arc out of nothing in an afternoon. *Where we've been* — the operational history of retail banking since the branch closures started, which he could do drunk. *Where we are* — the cost line, the third-party risk, the fact that every bank in the room was running its actual business on something written when the audience's junior staff were unborn. Both were written. Both were good; there was a joke in the second one about the mainframe that would land in a room like that the way a joke lands in a room that is frightened.

And then the third section, which every keynote of this kind has, and which the organiser had actually named for him in a bullet in a Word document: *A closing reflection — why we do this.*

He typed: *So why do we do this.*

He looked at it. He typed: *I've been asked that question a lot recently, and* — and deleted *recently*, because nobody had asked him, and typed *a lot over the years*, and then deleted the whole line.

He got up and made a coffee. At his desk with the coffee he tried the honest version, on the grounds that honest usually opened the door to something usable, and typed: *I do this because when I was thirteen a man put a sticker on our television.*

Which was true, and unusable, and also — this was the part that made him sit back and look out of the window at the top of a crane over towards Bishopsgate — no longer a complete answer.

Because there was a question underneath it that he had never once, in half a century, permitted onto the agenda, and it went like this. Suppose the boy had been asked. Suppose you could have sat the thirteen-year-old down at that table in Bushey and said: *right, here's the deal. You will never be poor. Nobody at your table will ever do that sum. In exchange, you will spend the years between thirty and sixty in rooms, and your daughter will grow up in the gaps between them, and you will become extremely good at something that will not, in the end, need you.*

Would the boy have taken it?

Yes, obviously. Instantly. The boy would have taken it before you finished the sentence.

*And was he right?*

That was the question, and Michael genuinely, sincerely, at sixty-four years old, with a document open in front of him and a conference in two weeks, did not know the answer. He could not get it to come out the same way twice. Some days he ran it and it came out *of course he was right, look at Claire, look at the house, look at the fact that nobody in this family has ever been frightened of a letter*; and other days he ran it and it came out — quietly, in the voice you use for things you are not going to act on — *he bought forty years of safety with the only forty years there were.*

A man who does not know why he does something cannot write four hundred words about it in a Word document.

He typed: *We do this because*

At 11:04 he got up and went to a meeting about card fraud, and at 11:52 he came out of it, and at 11:53 he stopped in the corridor with his hand on the wall and took a long breath in and let it out, and went to the next one.

---

Alan Weir got into the lift with him at three that afternoon, going down, holding a cardboard cup.

"You in the Atlas review this morning?"

"I was."

"Any good?"

"Callum's very good," said Michael.

"He is, isn't he." Alan pressed the button for the ground floor, which was already lit. "They're putting him up for the Group thing, apparently. The transformation office. Well — you'd know better than me."

Michael watched the numbers.

"I don't, actually," he said, pleasantly. "Nobody's mentioned it."

There was a floor and a half of silence, which in a lift is a considerable amount of silence, and then Alan said, "Ah, it's probably nothing. You know what it's like now, everything's decided in a call you're not on." He laughed. "Half my job is finding out what I've agreed to."

"That's the best half," said Michael. "The other half is doing it."

Alan laughed properly at that and got out still laughing.

Fiona Redgrave caught him at ten past four by the lifts on four. She was carrying her shoes, which she did about once a fortnight and which the entire fourth floor found so humanising that Michael had privately wondered for two years whether it was deliberate, and had concluded it was not, and that this was why it worked.

"You're doing the Ardenne thing."

"I am. Thank you for the biro."

"I meant it. They did ask for you by name. Colin somebody rang me up and said—" she did an impression, quite a good one, of a man being emphatic on a telephone "—*we'd like Halloway, he's the only one of you lot who tells the truth about operations.*"

"He should hear my forecasts."

"He probably has." She pressed the button. "How's the speech?"

"Going beautifully. Practically finished. I've written the whole of the middle and both edges."

Fiona laughed — a proper laugh, used economically, which made it worth something. "Michael. Can I say a thing."

"That's never a question."

"You have been — genuinely — the reason this place has held together for the last six years. I don't say it enough because you make a joke every time and then I feel silly. Atlas is a disaster in nine other banks and it's amber-green here, and the reason is that in 2013 you drew that thing on a whiteboard and refused to buy the American one." The lift came. She let it go. "You're the soul of this bank. Truly. Everyone knows it."

And Michael said, "Well, the soul doesn't have to do the RAG report," and Fiona laughed again and got in the next lift and went down.

He stood there.

The thing about a compliment, he thought, is that it has a tense.

*You have been.* Not *you are.* And *the soul of this bank* — he turned it over the way he had turned over a hundred board papers, looking for the load-bearing sentence, and found it: that a soul does not appear on an org chart, and cannot be assigned an outcome, and is not something you would consult about the Group transformation office, or the promotion of Callum Deitch, or, come to that, the shape of next year.

She had told him the truth. That was the trouble. She had told him, warmly and sincerely and in her own time, that he was deeply respected, and he ran the sentence twice more standing by the lifts and could not find, anywhere in it, the word *needed*.

He went back to his office and sat down and read the last line of the document, which said *We do this because*, and then closed the laptop, and put both hands flat on the desk, and took a breath in, and let it out.

Then he went down, and out through the revolving door into the evening, and on the pavement, out of sight of anyone from the bank, he stopped and took another one, and went to find his train.
