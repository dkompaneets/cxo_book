# 18. That's Nice, Nik

Sunday was a double-round day, and Nikhil Raghavan won both of them.

He won round four in the morning against a very solid man from Nuneaton who had beaten him in 2023, and he won it with a piece sacrifice on move twenty-two that he had not entirely calculated. That was the part he kept coming back to. He had sat there with eleven minutes on his clock and had seen four moves of it and had thought *and if it doesn't work,* and had then, for the first time in about six years, thought: *then it doesn't work.*

It worked.

He won round five in the afternoon in eighty minutes against a junior who played the opening from a video and had no idea what to do when it ran out.

Between the two he ate a jacket potato in the atrium café with Wilf Tanner, who had drawn both his games and was disgusted about it, and who said four things about the Nuneaton game, all of them technical, and one thing about the sacrifice, which was: "You'd have played Rc1 on Thursday."

"I know."

"Mm," said Wilf, and ate his soup.

At 5:20, walking out of the playing hall into a corridor with a window in it, Nik discovered that he was — the word arrived and he inspected it — *happy*, and that he had been happy for most of a day, and that this had last happened at some point that he could not date.

He rang home at six.

---

Meera picked up on the fourth ring with a noise behind her that was Rohan doing something to a saucepan.

"Hi. Hang on. *Rohan.* Hi."

"Is it a bad time?"

"It's six o'clock, it's always a bad time. How's the chess?"

And Nikhil Raghavan, standing in a corridor with a window in it, at the end of the best day he had had in a year, opened his mouth and heard himself say:

"Really good, actually. I won both. I'm in the top group now."

There is no top group at the Midlands Open. There is one Swiss, and after five rounds Nik was on four points out of five, which put him equal second on the live standings on a laptop by the arbiter's desk, and equal second is a true and excellent thing that he could have said in seven words.

"That's brilliant," said Meera. "Wait — both? In one day?"

"Two rounds today. And the morning one was against a guy who's beaten me before, and he's — I mean, he's rated about two hundred points above where I am." He was not. He was rated forty points above. "So that's a proper scalp."

"Nik, that's amazing."

"And I've been playing well. Like — actually well. Attacking." *That part was true.* "The hotel's a bit grim but there's a decent restaurant and I've been going to bed at eleven, so I've had, like, thirty-five hours of sleep in four days."

He had had about four and a half hours a night. He had been up until one on Thursday with a boy at a chessboard and until half past twelve on Friday. The hotel restaurant was fine. He had eaten in it once.

"You sound completely different," said Meera.

"I feel completely different."

"Good." A crash somewhere. "*Rohan.* — Sorry. And there's people to talk to? You're not just sitting in the room?"

And here it came.

"Yeah, no, it's been really social," said Nik. "There's a whole crowd of us — there's an old boy who's a retired engineer, he's fantastic, and I've been having dinner with a group of them, and there's a guy who's the CTO of a security startup, and a woman who's a CEO, and this COO from one of the big banks — we all went up on the roof on Friday and stayed up there half the night."

That was, sentence by sentence, entirely true, and by the time it had all been said in that order and in that voice it described a different weekend belonging to a different man: a weekend of dinners and a crowd, rather than one jacket potato, one accidental roof, and two conversations that had gone somewhere he had not intended.

"Wow," said Meera. "Look at you."

"I know."

"That's nice, Nik."

---

He did not notice it for about four seconds.

He was already going on — something about the standings, something about Tuesday — and there was a small delay, the way there is when you walk past a shop you used to know, and then the sentence came back round and hit him in the side of the head.

*That's nice, Nik.*

Flat. Warm — that was the thing, it was not cold, there was no edge on it at all. It was the exact tone in which you say *that's nice* to a four-year-old who has told you about a dream. It was pleasant, and complete, and it did not ask a single further question.

And Nik stood in the corridor with the window in it and heard it properly, and then heard, stacked up behind it like carriages, every other time.

*That's nice, Nik*, about the promotion in 2021.

*That's nice, Nik*, about the CEO ringing him personally.

*That's nice, Nik*, four years ago, when he had come home and told her about the funding round, and she had said it while looking for a shoe.

He had told himself for years that she was tired. She was tired; she was extremely tired; the tiredness was real and he had built a good deal on it. But a tired woman asks *and then what happened.* A tired woman says *hang on, say that again.*

She had stopped listening for the truth in it, because he had stopped putting it there. Not recently. Years ago. She had run the test — *is this the size he says it is* — some few hundred times, got the same answer, and at some point, without a decision and without a row, stopped running it.

He had done that. To the one person on earth who had married him knowing.

"Nik?"

"Sorry. I'm here."

"You went quiet."

"Signal," said Nik.

"Right." A pause, and then, in a different voice, the real one, the one from before: "Are you all right? You sound—"

"I'm fine. I'm great. I'm going to go and get some food."

"OK. Well done today. Genuinely." And then, half off the phone: "*Rohan. Give it to Mummy.*"

She went.

---

He did not go and get food.

He went up to room 812 and sat down at the little desk with the laptop and opened *2020 household v3*, because he had promised himself in a kitchen in Woking that he would do it properly at the hotel, and because after twenty minutes in a corridor he was in a state where doing the thing properly seemed suddenly possible, even attractive — a clean, cold, adult act.

He opened the credit card. He typed the number into the cell.

Nothing exploded. The twelve-month view at the bottom went from a small positive to a number in brackets, and the cell that said *closing balance, month 12* turned red because he had written the conditional formatting himself in 2020.

He looked at it for a minute. Then he began to work.

He ran it four times.

**Run one.** As is. Chennai at nine hundred a month, plus the one-lakh-sixty for the physiotherapy in August. Anaya's school at eleven-forty. The holiday in Croatia in the last week of August, which was four thousand two hundred all in, and which had been booked in January and paid for in two instalments of which one had been paid. Result: they ran out in the third week of September, and the card went up by about three thousand, and the twelve-month closing was in brackets by five figures.

**Run two.** Cancel Croatia. He got the cancellation terms up in another tab: fifty per cent retained after the first of July. So cancelling recovered two thousand one hundred pounds and cost the family the only week of the year in which Meera did not do bedtime alone. Result: it worked. It worked to the tune of a hundred and forty pounds a month of slack.

**Run three.** Keep Croatia, take Anaya out of the school. Eleven-forty a month, straight out, from September. The model went green so hard it was almost funny. He sat and looked at it and then wrote, in the notes column, in the language of a professional: *state alternative available from Sept, wait list, mid-year entry.* And then he sat and thought about the actual thing, which was a nine-year-old girl being told, in August, that she would not be going back to where Sofia and Nell and the whole of her life were, and being told it by a father who earned a hundred and eleven thousand pounds a year.

**Run four.** Cut Chennai. Nine hundred to four hundred, and no physiotherapy package, which meant the government hospital, which meant nine months on a knee that was already stopping his father from walking to the end of the road. He typed it in. He looked at the green cells.

He sat back.

There was no version. That was the finding, and he was a man who respected a finding. There was no arrangement of these numbers in which his father got his knee, his daughter kept her school, and his wife got the one week, and it did not matter how many times he ran it, because it was not a modelling problem, it was an arithmetic one, and he had been avoiding it since March in the specific way that only a man who is extremely good at arithmetic can avoid arithmetic.

He was the chief financial officer of a company with eleven million pounds in the bank. Nine people had listened to him on a call ten days ago and gone away reassured.

It was 8:52 on a Sunday evening and he could not afford his own life.

---

The message came at 8:56.

*Meera: The school payment's been declined. They've emailed. It says the direct debit was returned on Friday and there's a £25 charge and it has to be cleared by Thursday or she can't start the term. It's £1,140.*

*Meera: Nik there isn't £1,140 in the joint account. There's £310.*

*Meera: What's going on?*

He sat completely still.

The transfer had gone on Wednesday. Seventeen hundred pounds, to Chennai, out of the joint account instead of the other one because he had done it standing in a kitchen at eleven at night, in the app, on the phone, with the reference typed in as *ROUTINE.*

And Nikhil Raghavan sat at a small desk in room 812 of The Ardenne, with a spreadsheet open in front of him in which the numbers had, at some point in the last hour, stopped being numbers, and a phone in his hand, and a suitcase against the wall with a name tape sewn into the lid, and outside the window the car park and the ring road and the aircraft going round.

Somewhere below him, four floors down, a hundred and seventy-six people were putting the pieces back on the boards for the morning.
