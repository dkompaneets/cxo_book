# 19. A Profile Very Like His

He rang home at half past nine on the Sunday, and Claire answered, because Sunday was Ruth's swimming and Claire was there.

"Dad? Twice in one week."

"I know. I'm going peculiar."

"How's Birmingham?"

"Birmingham is enormous and beige and there's a chess tournament in the lobby. Listen — I wanted to ask about the twenty-seventh."

"The twenty-seventh?"

"Iris. The recital. St Barnabas."

"Oh!" said Claire, and then, "Oh — no, Dad, don't worry, it's fine."

"No, I know, I just — I've looked at the calendar and there's nothing, so I thought I'd—"

"Honestly, don't. It's a church hall and it's forty minutes and half of it is a boy called Rufus doing 'Für Elise' at a speed nobody has ever attempted. It's not a thing."

"I'd like to come."

There was a pause of about a second and a half, and it was in that second and a half — before she said anything at all — that Michael, who had spent forty-two years reading rooms for a living, understood that something was about to be said that he was not going to be able to put down.

"Dad," said Claire, kindly, warmly, without a single grain of anger in it, "we figured you couldn't. It's fine. We didn't even put you down for a seat."

---

He said, "Right."

"They only give you four," said Claire, "and it's me and Danny and Mum and Sam, and Sam won't sit anyway, so honestly, it's—"

"No, of course."

"If you're around we'll just — I'll ask, they might do a fifth, but they were quite—"

"Don't ask anybody for anything," said Michael. "That's completely fine. That's — you were right."

"Was that all right? I didn't mean—"

"It was entirely all right. Give her my love. Tell her I want a recording, and tell her that if there's a boy called Rufus playing 'Für Elise' too fast I want that in the recording as well, uncut, because I intend to be unkind about it."

Claire laughed. "I'll ring you tomorrow."

"Do."

He put the phone down on the bed.

Then he sat on the end of it in room 402 with his hands on his knees, and looked at the wall, and did the arithmetic, because he was constitutionally incapable of not doing arithmetic.

There had been no fight. That was the whole of it. There was not, anywhere in that conversation, a single thing to appeal against. There was no accusation, no reproach, no *you never came to anything*, no held breath, no woman on the other end of a phone saying *well, it's a bit late now, Dad.* If there had been, he could have done something with it. He had spent his working life in rooms where somebody was angry, and an angry room is a room where you still matter.

Claire had not been angry because Claire had finished. Some time ago — five years, eight, he had no idea and would never find out, because the moment had not been marked, because these moments never are — his family had run out of the version of him that came, and had rebuilt itself, competently, around the version that did not. They had four seats and four people. The arrangement was stable. It had been stable for years. It was not a wound they were nursing; it was a shape they had settled into, the way a house settles, the way a village builds its road around a hill.

He had not been excluded. He had been *routed around*, in the way you route around weather: without resentment, as a fact about the world.

He had taught Claire to swim. He wanted that noted, and he noted it, sitting on the end of a hotel bed in Birmingham with the phone in his hand. He had. In 1993, at the pool in Bushey, with a float, and he had probably said something encouraging, and she had probably swallowed a good deal of water.

He could not remember a single specific afternoon of it.

He got up at twenty to ten and looked at himself in the mirror of a hotel bathroom for slightly too long, and then took a breath in, and let it out, and went and put on a clean shirt, because at two o'clock he had to give a speech.

---

The keynote went well.

The Warwick Suite held four hundred and there were perhaps two hundred and eighty in it, which for the closing slot of a four-day conference is a triumph, and the man who introduced him mispronounced the bank's name.

Michael was funny for six minutes, and then serious for eleven, and then funny again, and the eleven serious minutes were the best public speaking he had done in a decade. He did the history of retail operations since 1986 and did not once mention transformation. He said the sentence about the mainframe and got the laugh he had banked on, and it went up the room like a wave, because every single person in that hall was running their actual business on something written before the audience's juniors were born, and nobody had said it out loud in four days.

He did nine minutes on the Ledger Gap, honestly, with the eleven million pounds in it and the man from Bristol left out, and at the end of that section a woman in the fourth row put her hand up in a keynote, which does not happen, and asked a real question, and he answered it and she wrote it down.

And then he came to the end, and did the thing he had planned in the shower, which was to say:

"They asked me for a closing reflection on why we do this. I've been trying to write it for three weeks and I couldn't, and about four o'clock this morning I worked out that this was because it's the wrong question, and the right question is *who did you tell.*" A beat. "Because the whole of our job, all of it, every day, is that we find out what's actually happening and then we tell somebody. That's it. That's the trade. And the reason it's hard is not that finding out is hard. Finding out is the easy half."

It was good. It got a proper hand.

He came off at 2:41 and eleven people wanted to talk to him and he was charming to all of them, and a man from a building society in the north-west said, "That was the only useful thing anyone's said all week," and shook his hand with both of his.

And Michael went back to room 402 at 3:20 and sat down and thought: *there was no paragraph about why we do this.*

He had written round it. He had done forty minutes and taken the exact question he had been asked and turned it, with great skill and real charm, into a different one that he could answer — and two hundred and eighty operations people had applauded, and eleven had queued, and one had said it was the only useful thing all week, and not a single human being in that room had noticed that a man had stood in front of them for forty minutes and declined to say why he had spent his life doing this.

Because he still did not know.

---

Terry Nwosu rang at twenty past six.

Michael was in The Long Room by then with a gin and tonic and the conference programme, in the good chair at the end of the bar, and he looked at the screen and felt, for about a second, straightforwardly pleased.

He had hired Terry in 2003 into a job Terry was not ready for, over the objection of two people, and had then spent four years quietly making sure he did not drown. Terry now ran the whole of the retail estate at a bank three times the size of Lauriston. They spoke perhaps twice a year, and Terry sent a case of wine at Christmas that was always slightly too good.

"Terrence."

"Michael. Where are you? It sounds like a bar."

"It is a bar. I've just given the closing keynote at the Banking Operations Forum, which is a sentence I'd like you to sit with."

"Was it any good?"

"It was superb, and I shall be forgotten by Thursday."

Terry laughed. Then he did not say anything for a moment, and Michael put his glass down on the bar mat, because he had known Terry Nwosu for twenty-two years and there were perhaps four things that man did with a silence and this was the bad one.

"Go on," said Michael.

"Right. OK." Terry breathed out. "I'm going to tell you a thing, and I want to say up front: I don't know it. I want that on the record. I don't know it, and it may be absolutely nothing."

"Understood."

"Marguerite Bell rang me on Thursday. She's at Harrow Vance now, doing exec search — she was at Ridgeway when we used her in 2018."

"I know Marguerite."

"She rang me to sound me out about someone. Not for a job — she wanted a reference on a candidate, and the candidate's someone I worked with at Lloyds, and it's fine, it's a good reference, I gave it." Terry paused. "And at the end I said, out of politeness, 'What's the role?' And she said she couldn't say, and then she said — and this is the whole of it, Michael, this is everything I have — she said, 'It's a group operations role at a mid-size UK bank, and it's a confidential one, so I can't.'"

The bar hummed. Denny put a glass down somewhere.

"And you thought of me," said Michael.

"I thought: there are eleven banks that fit that. And then I thought about the profile she was asking about — early fifties, big transformation background, ran a core migration, spent two years in the Group transformation office of a competitor — and I thought, that isn't a hire, that's a *replacement*, because nobody builds that spec for a new seat."

"No," Michael agreed. "They don't."

"It might be Skipton. It might be four other places. It might be a first-line risk job with a stupid title." Terry's voice was tight. "I nearly didn't ring. Amara said don't ring, you'll put a thing in his head for six months and it'll be nothing."

"You were right to ring."

"Was I?"

"Yes," said Michael. "You were right to ring, and I'd have done the same, and thank you, and now we are going to talk about something else, because there are exactly two things I can do with this information and neither of them is available on a Monday evening in a hotel in Birmingham."

They talked about something else for eleven minutes. Terry's eldest had got into Durham. Michael said the correct things and meant them.

"Michael," said Terry, at the end.

"Mm."

"Are you all right?"

"I'm marvellous," said Michael. "I've had a lovely day. I taught four hundred bankers about the 1994 core ledger and a woman in row four asked me a question."

"Right," said Terry, and let him have it, because he had known him twenty-two years and there was no other way this had ever gone.

---

Michael put the phone face down on the bar.

He picked up the gin and tonic and found that it was empty and that he did not remember it becoming empty.

"Denny," he said. "Same again, and then I want you to tell me something. How long have you worked here?"

"Nine years."

"Nine years. Right. So in nine years you have watched several thousand men in this exact chair receive a phone call and then order another one, and I want to know what the record is."

Denny grinned. "For drinks, or for phone calls?"

"For the interval between the two."

"About four seconds," said Denny. "It was a man from a print company. He didn't take the phone away from his ear."

"Ha!" said Michael, and it came out of him at full volume, the bark with the rise on the end, the completely convincing noise made by a professional, and it went across the bar and reached a group of banking delegates by the window, one of whom turned round and smiled at him — a nice man, a man from a building society in the north-west, who had said this afternoon that it was the only useful thing anyone had said all week.

And Michael heard the laugh die. He heard it go out flat across four metres of carpet and land on nothing, in an empty bar at twenty to seven, and he sat with the sound of it still in the room and understood, with the same clean administrative clarity with which he had once understood a set of accounts, that he had made a joke to a barman about the phone call in which he had been told he was being replaced, and that there was nobody at all in the building he was going to tell.

Then he remembered that there was a drink at nine, with three people whose surnames he did not know.

"Denny," he said. "Make it a large one."
