# 4. The Exchange Rate

There is a kind of true that is not honest, and Nikhil Raghavan had made a career of it.

He stood at the end of the long table in the games room — Lodestone had no boardroom, because Gus Delaney believed boardrooms made people behave like a board — with the fund on the screen behind him and a slide with four numbers on it, and every one of the four numbers was correct.

"Cash at quarter-end, eleven-two," he said. "Runway on current burn, fourteen months. That's before Sunward lands in November, and if that lands on time we're at nineteen."

"Nineteen months," said Gus, to the room, with satisfaction.

"If it lands on time," said Nik, and moved to the next slide before anyone could weigh the *if*.

What he did not say, and what was also true, was that the eleven-two included two-point-four of prepayments a publisher could ask back if Sunward slipped past November; that the runway assumed a headcount already several people short, and holding a role open makes runway look longer the way skipping meals makes money last; and that Sunward had never, in three attempts, landed on time. None of it was a lie. All of it sat in the pack, on page nineteen, in a table, correctly labelled, where Nik had put it himself so that it would exist quietly.

He said *healthy* once, about cash. He said *we're comfortable* about the Q3 position. Each did the work of a small load-bearing wall.

Then Bridget — the non-executive who had run a studio in Dundee in the nineties and was the only one who read the appendix — said: "Nikhil. The clawback on Sunward. That's the thirtieth of November, is that right?"

And Nik felt his whole body go still and warm, the way it does when the thing you have arranged to be invisible turns out to have been visible the entire time.

"That's right. Page nineteen."

"It's a big number."

"It is."

"But you're relaxed."

There was one second, perhaps a second and a half, in which he could have said: *No. I've been awake at four in the morning about this since March, I think the odds are about even, and we should be talking to the publisher now rather than in October.*

He watched Gus's face on the screen — Gus, who had made a game about a rabbit and had put his own house into this company in 2016.

"I'm watchful," said Nik. "That's my job. But yes. Relaxed."

"Good," said Bridget. "Then I am."

Afterwards Gus rang him, on the phone, not the video, which he did about twice a year. "That was excellent. Honestly. You just — you make it feel like there's a floor under everything. I don't know how you do it."

Nik sat with the phone against his ear and looked at the car park.

"Practice," he said.

---

The rest of the day arrived, the way it always did, as numbers.

The bank, renewing the Dublin overdraft at forty basis points worse than last year. A forecast revision from Priyesh, who was twenty-eight and thorough and had caught a marketing accrual sitting in the wrong quarter since March. A purchase order for a QA vendor, forty-one thousand, approved on his phone in a queue in Pret. An email from HR wanting the salary bands finalised by Friday, which he could not do, because finalising them meant telling nine people a number and he had not decided which of two numbers to tell them.

And at 14:38, a text from his mother in Chennai: *Nikhil the appointment is on 3rd. Appa says it is nothing. It is not nothing.*

He read that one four times. His father's knee had been going for two years. The private hospital on Greams Road would do it in a week; the government one in nine months; and the difference between those two things was about seventeen hundred pounds — he did the exchange in his head instantly, without wanting to, the way he did every time — which was two-thirds of what he sent them in a normal month, on top of what he sent them in a normal month.

*I'll send it this week*, he typed. *Tell Appa he has to do the exercises after, not just before.*

At a red light on the A3 he signed a finance approval on his phone, six thousand two hundred, the studio's Christmas thing, booked in July because the venue was cheaper in July. He knew it was illegal to sign it at the light. He did it anyway, because the app would have logged it as *pending* overnight and Gus would have seen *pending* and asked.

---

The commute was fifty-one minutes on a good day and had been the honest hour of his life for four years, because nobody could reach him on the A3, and there was a stretch after the Wisley interchange where the phone lost signal for ninety seconds, and Nik had come to look forward to those ninety seconds the way other men look forward to a drink.

He did the real arithmetic there — not the studio's, his own — and it did not improve with acquaintance.

Take-home, after tax and pension, just under seven thousand a month. Rent on the house in Woking, twenty-one fifty, a house not a flat, three bedrooms, because Anaya needed a room and Rohan needed a room and Meera had asked for neither; Nik had decided them. Anaya's school, eleven-forty a month, a thing he had chosen when she was six and had never been able to undo without explaining to a nine-year-old why she was leaving her friends. Chennai, nine hundred in a normal month. The car, the insurance, the nursery days, the food shop that had climbed from a hundred and sixty a week to two hundred and thirty without anyone agreeing to it.

That left, in a good month, four hundred pounds. Not four hundred of savings — four hundred of *slack*, the margin that stands between a family and an event. The boiler was eleven years old. There was no cushion, nowhere to fall.

He was the chief financial officer of a company with eleven million pounds in the bank, and he was seventeen hundred pounds short, and there was no one on earth he could tell. He checked it for self-pity every time, the way you check a bag for keys, and every time it came back as simply a fact about the shape of his life: tell Gus, and he was a CFO with money trouble, a phrase that ends careers regardless of the arithmetic behind it; tell Meera the real version, and she would go back to work with Rohan at three, and it would cost more in nursery than it earned, and she would do it anyway, and he would have to watch.

---

He had learned the trick at about twenty-two, and it had cost nothing at the moment of use, which was the whole of the trap.

There had been five years — 1999 to about 2004 — when he said a sentence and there was a beat, and the other person's face did a small thing, a fractional leaning-in, and then said "Sorry?", and he said it again, slightly louder, and it arrived in the room four seconds late and a little smaller than the sentences around it. Four seconds and ten per cent, forty times a week, for five years, does something to a man's relationship with speech.

He tried going quiet first. That cost him a promotion in 2005 to a man called Duncan who was worse than him and had said four thousand things to Nik's sixty. So he tried the other thing, and the other thing worked at once, which was the disaster: if you took a true thing and pressed it — not falsified, *pressed*, the way you press a shirt — it landed, and when it landed people stopped leaning in, and when they stopped leaning in they stopped saying "Sorry?", and the four seconds went away.

He had come over in September 1999 with two suitcases bought on the Mount Road in Chennai, cheap hard shells, one grey and one maroon, and his mother had sewn a fabric name tape inside the maroon lid because a woman at the temple had told her that bags get swapped at Heathrow. His father had come to the airport in the good shirt and said very little, and then at the gate had pushed an envelope down into Nik's shirt pocket — into the pocket, not the hand, so it could not be given back — and pressed his palm flat over it, and said, in Tamil, *"Don't call for money. Call for anything else."*

Twenty-six years. He had never once called for money.

---

Meera was on the sofa with the baby monitor on the arm and her eyes shut, not asleep, resting with the specific tension of a woman who expects to be needed in ten minutes.

"He went down at seven. He'll be up at nine, and again at one." She opened her eyes. "How was work?"

"Good. Busy. Gus is happy about Sunward."

"Is Sunward going to be fine?"

"Sunward's going to be fine."

She nodded slowly, and looked at him a beat longer than the answer needed, and said, "There's dal. It's not very good. Rohan put a car in it."

They ate. Anaya came down in pyjamas to argue about a school trip, and Nik said yes before he had heard the price, and then heard the price, and said yes again in a slightly different voice, and Anaya, who was nine and had radar, said, "It's fine if it's too much," and Nik said, "It isn't too much," and it was a hundred and forty-five pounds.

---

He packed on the Wednesday evening, and the maroon suitcase came down off the wardrobe with a smell that was thirty years old.

The grey one had died in 2014. The maroon one was still going: shell scratched matte, one corner crushed by a baggage system in Frankfurt in 2009, the name tape still legible in Shobha Raghavan's stitching, and the front left wheel dragging, so the whole case pulled to one side and had to be leaned against, like a difficult dog.

Anaya sat on the end of the bed with her chin on her fists and watched him fold shirts into it.

"Appa. Why don't you just buy a new one."

"There's nothing wrong with this one."

"The wheel's broken."

"The wheel isn't broken, the wheel is worn."

"That's the same thing." She got up and pulled it two feet across the carpet, and it went sideways into the wardrobe door. "See. In Dubai they have suitcases with four wheels. You just push them. They go anywhere."

"When have you been to Dubai?"

"Sofia went to Dubai."

"Ah," said Nik. "Sofia."

She flumped back onto the bed. "It's not even a nice colour."

And he opened his mouth to say the thing — *this case came here with me, this case has been in this country longer than your mother, my mother sewed that tape in with her hands at a table in a flat you have never seen* — and heard, in advance, exactly how it would land: the immigrant father, the parable, the story with a moral on the end, delivered to a girl in a school uniform who had been asked to feel something on cue. He had done that twice and watched her face do the small polite arrangement both times.

"It's maroon," he said. "It's a very good colour. Go and do your reading."

"It's *brown*," said Anaya from the door, having got the last word, which was fair; she had won on the merits.

He stood in the bedroom with a shirt in his hands for a moment. Then he went downstairs and moved seventeen hundred pounds to Chennai out of the account he was not supposed to use for it, and typed, in the reference box, as he did every month, the word *ROUTINE*, so that if Meera ever looked at the statement it would sit in the column with all the others and not raise its hand.

---

At eleven, the house quiet and Rohan not yet awake, Nik sat at the kitchen table with the laptop and did not open the family budget.

He opened, instead, the registration page for the Midlands Open. Five days at a hotel outside Birmingham called The Ardenne, seven rounds across the long weekend, entry forty-eight pounds. He had played it twice. It was the only thing in his year he did not exaggerate about, in either direction — he did not tell people at work he played chess, because he could not have borne the way they would find it charming, and he did not tell his mother he was going, because she would tell the neighbours, and by the second telling he would be in a tournament in London and by the fourth he would be winning it.

The form asked for a name. The box said FULL NAME AS IT WILL APPEAR ON THE PAIRING SHEET, and Nik sat with his hands over the keys, and then typed the whole thing, slowly:

*Nikhil Raghavan.*

Nobody had called him Nikhil at work in six years. He had shortened it himself, in 2018, in a meeting in Reading where a man had said "Nick? Nick-hill? Sorry, mate—" and Nik had said "Nik's fine," and it had been fine, and it had cost him something so small he had not noticed the transaction at the time and had thought about it perhaps a thousand times since.

He paid the forty-eight pounds. Then he set up a position on the board in his head — the one he had lost to an old man two years ago from a winning position, because he had spent forty minutes making sure he could not lose — and played it through, both sides, in silence, until upstairs a small voice began to complain about the dark.
