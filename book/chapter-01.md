# 1. Twelve Minutes

The calendar at Bellwether ran on twelve-minute blocks because Kate Merrick had once read that the medieval English penny was divided into twelve, and had liked the idea of a unit that could be split three ways or four without a remainder. That was the reason she gave. The real reason was that a half-hour meeting will always take a half hour, and a twelve-minute meeting is over before anyone has finished being pleased to see you.

At 9:24 on the Monday she was in a room she had no business being in.

It was a sprint review for the returns platform, four levels below her, in the small room on the second floor that everyone called the Aquarium because three of its walls were glass and the fourth had a print of a whale on it. Nine people sat around a table built for eight. When Kate came in at 9:23 there was that half-second of rearrangement, the tiny weather change of a room discovering that the CEO has joined the sprint review, and a young man called Otis stood up to give her his chair, and she said *no, no, honestly*, and took it.

Priyanka from engineering was walking them through the queue depth on the refund service, and Kate was watching a dashboard on her phone under the table.

The dashboard was the one they had built for her in her first year, when Bellwether had been two brands and a warehouse in Erith, and it showed twelve numbers on a black background. Ten of them were green. One was amber, which meant nothing; the amber threshold had been set by a contractor in 2021 and had been slightly wrong ever since, and every quarter someone said they should fix it and every quarter it stayed. The twelfth was red.

Kate looked at the red number for two seconds, which was as long as she could look at anything.

"Sorry," she said, and everyone stopped. "Sorry. Go back one. The four hundred and twelve — is that four hundred and twelve refunds queued, or four hundred and twelve customers?"

"Refunds," said Priyanka.

"So some of those are the same person."

"Some of them are the same person."

"How many?"

There was a pause that Kate recognised because she caused it perhaps forty times a day. It was the pause of a competent person discovering the exact edge of what they had prepared.

"I'd have to check," said Priyanka.

"It'll be about one-point-four," Kate said. "It's always one-point-four. So we're annoying two hundred and ninety-odd people, not four hundred, which is a different meeting." She was already standing. "Tom, can you and I do six minutes on this at eleven? Not you, Gareth, you've got the board thing."

Gareth Pryce, the CTO, who was sitting two chairs down and had not been asked anything, said, "Sure."

She was out of the Aquarium at 9:31, three minutes over, which meant she was three minutes over for the rest of the day.

---

The thing about the corridor was that it was the only place she was ever alone, and she had learned to use it. She walked with the phone at her mouth like a sommelier smelling a cork.

"Dawn — hi — can you find me twelve minutes with Tom before eleven, and if it has to eat the Ocean Group call then push Ocean to Thursday but tell them it's because I want to give it proper time, which is true. Also I need twenty with Harriet before the summit, and I know there isn't any, so." She paused at the lift. "So find it."

She sent it. Somewhere on the second floor a phone buzzed on Dawn Fowler's desk beside a photograph of two children on a beach in weather that was clearly not warm.

At 10:52 she was in Tom Ferreira's part of the floor, standing by his desk rather than sitting, because standing kept it short. Tom was the VP of Engineering. Gareth was the CTO. On the org chart there was a clean line from Kate to Gareth and from Gareth to Tom, and in the physical world Kate walked past Gareth's office, which had a glass door and Gareth behind it, to get to Tom's desk.

"Talk to me about the refund queue," she said.

Tom talked to her about the refund queue. He was good. He was, she thought, genuinely better at this than Gareth was, which was an unhelpful thought and one she had trained herself to have privately. In nine minutes they found it: a retry policy doing exactly what it had been told to do, and told the wrong thing eighteen months ago by a person who no longer worked there.

"Ship it today," Kate said.

"Gareth's got a freeze on until after the summit."

"Ship it today," Kate said, "and I'll tell Gareth."

She did not tell Gareth. She meant to. It went onto the list she kept in her head of things she would do when there was a gap, a list which by now had the structure and permanence of geology.

---

The launch slipped on the Wednesday, and she found out in the fourth bullet of a status email at 7:02, under a heading that said *Refill — update*.

*Following the payments review on 26/6, Refill launch moves to 9 September to accommodate the migration to Marrowbone. Comms updated. No change to Q4 forecast.*

She read it twice, standing in the kitchen with no coffee made, and the thing she noticed was not the five weeks. It was the date. *The payments review on 26/6.* A review had happened, a real one, with minutes and a decision in it, and she was learning about it now, in a bullet, because to the nine people in that room there had been nothing to tell.

She had been in Rotterdam on the twenty-sixth of June.

By 7:20 she had established that the decision was right, which was the part that frightened her. Marrowbone was better; the incumbent had failed twice in June and their tokenisation would become a problem in Germany by spring. Gareth had run it, Tom had been in it, the analysis was sound. And it moved the single biggest thing Bellwether was doing this year by five weeks, and nobody had thought to walk it past the person who owned the number it landed on, because owning the number was not on anyone's job description except hers.

She stood at the island and put her weight, without deciding to, onto the ball of her left foot, and turned it out a few degrees, heel down. Her body did this about four times a year, at sinks, in lifts, on station platforms, and she never caught it until it had already happened. She caught it now, and stopped, and made the coffee.

---

She had danced until she was eleven. Tuesdays and Saturdays, a church hall in Amersham that smelled of hymn books and radiators, a Russian woman called Mrs Volkova who shouted and smelled of hairspray, and Kate somewhere in the middle of the class — good, she thought, though there was no one left to ask — in the second half, when her body stopped asking permission.

There was one dinner she still had, whole, the way you keep a photograph you didn't choose to take.

She was nine. She had come to the table with a certificate — a real one, distinction, her name in a teacher's italic — and her mother had read it and been delighted, genuinely, warmly, the whole table lifting a degree with the pleasure of it, and had said, "Katherine. That's wonderful." And then, still smiling, in the same breath, reaching for the water: "And how's the maths coming? Because it's the maths that's the mountain, isn't it."

There was no cruelty in it. Kate had spent four sessions with a therapist in 2019 defending exactly that — *they were warm, they were interested, nobody was ever cold* — in a voice she could hear going too fast. Her mother had not been withholding praise. She had simply, without any malice, moved the line the moment Kate reached it, because in that house the line was not a place you could arrive, it was a direction you faced.

The Saturday class went first, for the eleven-plus tutor. Then the tutor went well, and there was the scholarship, and the scholarship wanted maths, and maths was Tuesday. Nobody stopped her dancing. There was no scene. There was a diary, and a list of what mattered, and dancing slid quietly down it, and one afternoon in March she noticed she had not been in five weeks and that no one in the house had remarked on it.

She had not been to a class since. She was forty-one, and she could still, at a kitchen island at twenty past seven in the morning, feel exactly where her weight ought to be.

---

At 9:15 she had Gareth in her office and did it properly, because she had learned to do it properly.

"The decision's right," she said, and meant it. "The analysis is good, and I'd have made the same call. I'm not asking whether it's real. I'm asking who decided that five weeks of Refill was the price we were paying, and whether they knew what Refill was worth."

"It came out of the review, Kate. It's the migration window, we can't—"

"I know we can't. That's not the question." She heard herself and could not stop. "The question is that nine people moved the biggest thing we do this year, and not one of them owns the number it lands on, and nobody thought that was worth a phone call."

Gareth Pryce was forty-eight, had been a CTO at two companies before this one, and did not deserve the temperature in the room. "Then the process—"

"The process is fine. Nobody did anything wrong." Which was true, and was the most honest sentence she would say all week, and which Gareth, reasonably, heard as a threat.

After he left she stood at the window for a while, and then she sat down and opened the operating rhythm document, and did the thing.

She added herself to the weekly platform sequencing call — Gareth's call, Tuesdays, forty minutes. And she created a new item: a Thursday half-hour in which any decision that moved a date on a top-ten initiative got walked past her first.

She typed the second one with a specific physical relief, the feeling of a strap pulled tight. Then she added, at the bottom, *Temporary — through Q4*, and believed it, exactly as she had believed the identical line she had typed in November about the pricing forum, which she still chaired.

Dawn's reply came four minutes later. *That's two more standing commitments in a week where you asked me to find twenty minutes with Harriet and I took it out of your lunch. Saying it out loud.*

*Noted*, Kate wrote. *Do it anyway.*

*Doing it.*

At 2:00 Harriet Nash rang about the summit agenda, and in the last ninety seconds, in the voice she kept for weather and for warnings, said: "Everyone's terribly excited about Refill for the August peak."

Here was the door. There were two weeks to the summit and a chief executive who could have said, calmly, on a Wednesday afternoon: *it's moved to September, here's why, it's the right call — and while I've got you, twenty-two isn't going to happen, and I'd like to do that properly, with a plan, not badly, in a corridor.*

"There's a payments migration in front of it," Kate said. "I'll walk the board through the sequencing at the summit."

"Lovely," said Harriet. "You're a machine."

---

At 12:36 the following day, in a car going east, she recorded a voice message to Sasha and Bea.

She was good at these. Voice messages were the only communication she had left that had any of her actual voice in it, and she used them like a woman spending a currency about to be devalued.

"Right — so — I know. I *know*. Don't say anything, I can hear you both saying it. Here's the thing about Thursday." A lorry pulled out in front of them and the driver, Vasyl, made a small noise of professional contempt. "The summit runs Thursday to Sunday, and the board join at the end of it, and Harriet wants a pre-read a week out, which means the whole thing has to be finished by the fifth, which means Thursday is—" She stopped. She rewound nothing; the message just kept running. "Look. I'm not going to make Thursday. I'm going to make the next one. Actually don't book the next one around me, book it and I'll come, and if I don't come you're allowed to be properly angry rather than the nice kind of angry you both do, which is worse. I love you. I'm the worst. Bye."

She listened to it back before sending, which she never did with work messages, and then she sent it.

The replies came in before the Blackwall Tunnel. Sasha sent a GIF of a woman sighing. Bea sent: *third time K.* And then, four minutes later, because Bea was constitutionally unable to leave a thing sharp: *joking. come whenever. love you.*

Kate looked at *third time K* for a while, and then a notification came in over the top of it about the Ocean Group, and by the time she surfaced they were at the warehouse.

---

The problem, which she had not solved, and which was two weeks away, went like this.

Bellwether had grown twenty-two per cent a year for four years, and the board had accordingly written down a number for next year that assumed twenty-two per cent again, and the number was not achievable, and Kate knew it was not achievable, and had known since March. There were three ways to say so. She could say it early, which would look like managing expectations, which was the phrase people used to describe a chief executive beginning to fail. She could say it late, which was worse. Or she could bring a plan so obviously right that the number changed shape while everyone looked at the plan.

She did not have the plan. She had four days of work stacked behind the summit and a folder of half-built versions and the growing, unfamiliar, sickening sense that this was a problem she could not simply outwork.

She had never once, in twenty years, failed to outwork a problem.

---

At 5:02 Dawn Fowler put her coat on.

Kate saw it from across the floor and something in her went very still, the way it did about four times a year. Dawn packed her bag: a laptop she would not open, a lunchbox, a second lunchbox. The second lunchbox went onto Kate's desk on the way past.

"That's the aubergine thing," Dawn said. "You said you liked it. It's better the second day, so don't eat it tonight, eat it tomorrow, which you won't."

"I'll eat it tomorrow."

"You'll eat it at eleven o'clock tonight standing up." Dawn was already turning. "Marcus has got football and Elsie's got a thing where I have to buy a shoebox. Not shoes. The box." She stopped at the top of the stairs. "The Harriet twenty minutes — I've got you the eighth, eight-forty, but she's doing it from a car."

"That's fine."

"It's not fine, it's a car, but it's twenty minutes." Dawn looked at her for slightly longer than the exchange required. "Are you all right?"

"Fine," said Kate. "Big fortnight."

"Big fortnight," Dawn agreed, and went down the stairs, and out through the doors, and away into a life that had a shoebox in it.

Kate stood by the window and watched the top of Dawn's head cross the car park, and had a thought so unwelcome that she put it away before she had finished it — something about how one of them was leaving and one of them was staying and only one of them had a reason for it that would survive being said out loud.

Then her phone went, and it was Ocean Group, and the thought was gone.

---

At 9:40 the cleaners came out of the lift with the trolley, and the woman whose name Kate had asked for twice and lost twice said "All right?" and Kate said "All right," and the vacuum started up at the far end of the floor like weather coming in.

The tupperware sat on her desk, unopened. At 10:20 she ate it standing up.

She read a version of the plan she had written on Sunday and thought was good, and it was not good, it was a way of saying *we will try harder*, dressed up. She deleted a slide. She wrote a note to herself in the doc — *the number is wrong, say the number is wrong* — and then deleted that too, because documents in a company are not private, and because she was not ready to have written it down.

At 11:14 she opened the calendar for the following week and looked at it the way you look at a wall.

She typed: *Dawn — sorry, I know. Can we get Ocean and the ops review into next week somewhere.*

Then she deleted *sorry, I know*, because it was 11:14 at night and an apology would only make Dawn feel she had to reply.

She sent: *Dawn — Ocean and the ops review need to be next week. Anywhere.*

The message showed as delivered. Nobody read it, because the person it was sent to was asleep in a house with a shoebox in it.

Kate put the phone face down, and then, forty seconds later, turned it back over.
