# 22. The Message I Sent

Dawn Fowler's desk was on the second floor by the window, and it had on it: two screens, a keyboard with a wrist rest, a mug that said WORLD'S OKAYEST MUM, a photograph of two children on a beach in weather that was clearly not warm, and a plant that had been there since 2021 and was, by any reasonable measure, thriving.

Kate came down at 9:15 on the Wednesday morning.

She had rehearsed it in the car. She had rehearsed it for two days, and every version she built had the same architecture, because it was the only architecture she owned: an opening that established the context, a middle that acknowledged the impact, and a close that proposed a way forward. She had, at one point on the Tuesday evening, opened a document.

"Dawn," she said. "Have you got twenty minutes? Not — it's not a work thing. I need to say something and it needs a room."

Dawn looked up.

"Oh God," she said. "Am I being made redundant?"

"No. No. Christ, no. Opposite. Sort of."

They went into the small room on the second floor with the whale on the wall. Kate shut the door, and Dawn sat down with a notebook, which she always brought, and Kate looked at the notebook and something in her chest turned over.

"You won't need that."

"I always need it."

"Right," said Kate.

And then she found that every single one of the versions she had built was gone.

---

What she did instead was this. She took her phone out, and opened it, and found the message, and put the phone on the table between them face up, and pressed play.

"Kate—"

"Please just let it."

*Dawn — it's me. It's eight fifty-two on Sunday. I'm standing outside the board and the deck in the folder is the second of July version.*

It is a very strange thing to sit in a small room and listen to your own voice at that volume.

Kate had never once heard it. She had recorded four or five voice messages a day for six years and had listened back to perhaps three of them in her life, and what she had not understood — what she genuinely, in a way that would never be fully repaired, had not understood — was what the thing sounded like at the other end. In her head it had been a fast note dictated in a corridor. Out loud in a quiet room, at nine in the morning, it was a woman speaking very slowly and extremely clearly, without a single stumble, for ninety-one seconds, into a phone, to somebody who could not answer.

*I need you to understand what that means, so I'm going to say it once.*

Dawn was looking at the table.

*A job is a list of things and you do them and you go home at five with your tupperware and your shoebox.*

There was a small movement in Dawn's jaw.

*I have been carrying yours for two years and pretending it was mine.*

Kate made herself watch the whole of it. That was the only decision she made in the room and it was the only one that mattered: she did not look away, and she did not look at the phone, she looked at Dawn Fowler's face for the last forty seconds of a recording that she had made herself.

*Do not ring me back.*

It stopped.

The room did the thing rooms do.

"Right," said Kate. Her voice had gone. "I'm not going to explain any of it. I know I'm going to want to, in about nine seconds, so I'm telling you now that I'm not going to, because everything I could say is a way of making you carry it with me.

"There was no mistake. You did exactly what you said you'd do, and you told me in writing at twenty-two minutes to twelve on the Wednesday night that you couldn't get into my drive, and I read it and typed *Perfect*, and then on Sunday morning I decided, in about two seconds, that it was you. And it took me until eleven o'clock that morning to find out that it was me, and by then you'd played it. At fourteen minutes past nine on a Sunday morning with a sick child in the house.

"I'm sorry. Not for the pressure I was under, or the fortnight I'd had, or any of it. For what I did to you. I'm sorry."

She stopped, because there was nothing after it.

---

Dawn Fowler said: "Oh, Kate."

That was all. She said it the way you say it when somebody has dropped something.

"I mean — it was horrible," she said. "I'm not going to pretend. I cried in the airing cupboard, because Marcus was on the sofa and I didn't want him to see. And then Steve rang and I told him and he said something rude about you and I got quite defensive, which he thought was hilarious." She shrugged. "And then Monday I came in and did the Ocean file."

"That's—"

"You've been like a wire since March," said Dawn. "I've watched you not eat for four months. Honestly, I thought something was going to happen, I just thought it'd be your heart."

"That's not a reason."

"No," Dawn agreed, comfortably. "It's not a reason. It's just why I'm not surprised." She picked up the mug, which was empty. "It's fine. It's genuinely fine. Are we all right?"

And Kate Merrick, who had two hours ago been unable to eat, said, "Yes."

"Good. Do you want a coffee?"

"Dawn—"

"Because I'm having one, and if I go now I'll get the machine before the second floor does that thing at half nine."

And she got up, and went, and it had taken four minutes.

---

That was what took her apart.

Kate sat in the small room with the whale on the wall for ten minutes after Dawn had gone.

She had prepared for a difficult conversation. She was excellent at difficult conversations; she had done four hundred of them; there was a *shape* to a difficult conversation and Kate could take one from anywhere to anywhere in twenty minutes. She had come down the stairs armoured for cold politeness, or for tears, or for a resignation, or for a long negotiation in which she would have had, at some point, to do something skilful.

What she had got was a woman saying *oh, Kate*, and being sorry for her, and going to get a coffee.

There was no work available in it. Nothing to earn, or level, or close out, or put on a list. It had been handed over free, by a woman with a plant on her desk, and gone to get a coffee.

A board of seven had said *exceptional* eight days earlier and she had felt nothing at all.

She sat in the small room with her hand over her mouth.

---

The crisis broke on the Thursday at 3:40, which was the first properly load-bearing thing to happen since the board.

A supplier's certificate expired without anyone noticing, and every card payment on the mobile app stopped working, and it stopped working at a quarter to four on a Thursday in July.

Kate was on the second floor when Tom Ferreira found her.

"We've got—"

"I know," said Kate. "I've got the alert."

"So the fix is—"

"Tom." She put her phone in her pocket. That was the whole action, physically: she put a phone into a jacket pocket. "It's yours."

He looked at her.

"It's yours," she said again. "I'm not going to be in it."

"Kate, it's the *app*—"

"I know what it is."

"Do you want me to—"

"No."

And she walked away, which was the single hardest professional act of her career and which took her past the window where she could see him standing there.

It took two hours and twenty minutes and it was not clean. Tom wobbled at 4:20; there is a recording of a call where he says "I'll need to check with—" and then stops, audibly, and there are two seconds of dead air on the line, and then he says, "No. Do it." He got the sequence wrong at 5:05 and had to back one thing out. He made a call about customer comms at 5:30 that Kate, watching the channel from a room upstairs with her hands flat on a table, would not have made, and which was — she looked at it for a long time — defensible. Possibly better.

At 5:51 the app was back.

At 5:58 Tom put a message in the channel: *Back up. Root cause was the cert, we've got an alert on expiry now which we should have had since 2022. My call on the comms, I'll write it up. Sorry for the two hours.*

Kate read it four times.

He had wobbled, and held, and got there, and it had cost the business about twenty thousand pounds more than it would have cost if she had done it herself.

She thought about the twenty thousand pounds for a while.

Then she thought about what it had cost her, every time, for four years, to save it.

---

She rang her parents on the Sunday.

It was a fortnightly call and it had a structure that both parties had honoured since about 2011: her mother asked how work was, and Kate gave a version — genuinely a good version, funny, self-deprecating, containing at least one achievement placed where it would not look placed — and her mother said something warm and then said the next thing.

"So how's it all going?" said Elizabeth Merrick.

And Kate said: "It's fine. It's busy. How's the garden?"

There was a small pause.

"The garden's a disaster, the box has got something. But how's — what happened with the board thing? Wasn't there a board thing?"

"There was. It went fine." Kate was standing at her window. "Tell me about the box."

Her mother told her about the box. It took ten minutes and involved a man called Gordon who had a theory. Kate asked three questions about it, and did not report a single thing, and did not ask for a single thing, and at the end her mother said, "Well — you sound busy, darling," slightly puzzled, because the shape of the call had been wrong, and Kate said, "I am. Love to Dad," and put the phone down.

She sat on the sofa.

It was not forgiveness and it was not a confrontation, and nothing whatsoever had been resolved. It was simply that after thirty-two years, on a Sunday afternoon, for eleven minutes, she had stopped servicing the debt. Nobody had been told. Nobody had noticed. The debt was still there and would be there at Christmas.

---

The dance class was on Wednesdays at 6:30 in a hall behind a leisure centre in Bermondsey and was called *Adult Beginners / Improvers — Contemporary*, which Kate had read four times looking for a way out.

She booked it on the Monday. She nearly cancelled it on the Tuesday. On the Wednesday at 5:50 she left the office in front of forty people, which had not happened in nine years, and Dawn watched her go and said nothing.

At 6:14, running down Tooley Street with a bag, she got the call.

"Kate, sorry — it's Tom. The Marrowbone people are saying the settlement file—"

She stopped on the pavement.

There was a version of her, four weeks old, that was already turning round.

"Tom," she said. "My working day is over. Figure it out yourself."

There was silence on the line.

"...OK," said Tom.

"You'll be fine."

"Yeah. No, I — yeah."

She hung up and stood there on Tooley Street with her heart going, and then she laughed, out loud, on her own, and a man walking past looked at her, and she went to the class and was, for fifty minutes, the worst person in the room by a distance so comical that the teacher, a woman of about sixty called Ines, twice used the phrase "and Kate is *exploring*."

She could not do the turn. She could not do the thing with the arms at all. At one point, going the wrong way across the floor, she collided with a retired postman.

She came out at 7:35 with her hair wrecked and a graze on one elbow and her whole body ringing, and she stood in the car park behind the leisure centre in the July evening and thought: *nobody in that room knows what I do.*

---

She mentioned it on the Friday, in passing, to Dawn, because she had to tell somebody and there was nobody else.

"I've started a dance class."

Dawn looked up from her screen.

"Have you," she said.

"It's — I'm horrifically bad. Wednesdays."

Dawn took her glasses off.

"Kate," she said, "I've danced on Wednesdays for eight years."

There was a pause of about four seconds.

"You *what*?"

"Wednesdays. Half seven, at the church hall on Milkwood Road. It's ballroom and Latin and it's mostly people over fifty and there's a man called Colin who's genuinely nationally ranked and does not let you forget it." Dawn was smiling in a way Kate had never seen. "Eight years. It's why I go at five on a Wednesday."

Kate stood there.

"You've gone at five on a Wednesday for eight years," she said, "and I have never once asked you why."

"No," said Dawn cheerfully. "You haven't."

And then she said: "Do you want to come?"
