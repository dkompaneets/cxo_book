# 14. The Second Board

She found him on the Saturday afternoon in the worst chair in the building.

The Ardenne had a first-floor landing with a window that looked down into the atrium, and somebody in 1998 had put two armchairs there facing the glass, and nobody had sat in them since, because they faced the wrong way and there was a draught. Michael was in one of them with a conference programme he was not reading, watching a hundred and seventy-six people play chess in silence four metres below him.

"This is a terrible chair," said Kate.

"It's a magnificent chair. It's the only place in this hotel where nobody can find you."

"I found you."

"Yes," said Michael, "well," and moved the programme off the other one.

The board had arrived at five for dinner. Kate had two hours. She had spent nine of them on Friday and eleven on Saturday morning being certain in front of sixty-one people, and she had come up the stairs to find a quiet place to read the deck for the ninth time, and had seen him through the glass and had not read the deck.

They watched the chess for a while. From above it looked like a system: a hundred and seventy-six people bent over eighty-eight boards under the strip lights, and every so often, somewhere in the grid, one of them would stand up and stretch and walk about, and you could tell from the walk who was winning.

"Can I ask you something properly?" said Kate.

"That's ominous."

"It is."

Michael put the programme down.

---

"If I do this well," said Kate, "what happens?"

"Do what well?"

"This. The thing I'm doing. Say I'm right about everything. Say Refill lands in September and does what it's supposed to do, and say the board takes fourteen on Sunday and doesn't put a knife in me for it, and say we do fourteen and then twelve and then we buy someone and I run it, and it works. Say I don't fail." She was looking down at the boards. "What happens? At the end?"

"Of the company?"

"Of me."

Michael did not answer immediately.

"How old are you?" he said.

"Forty-one."

"Right." He looked at the atrium. "So the honest answer is that you'll be extremely good at it. That's not a compliment, it's a diagnosis. In about four years you'll notice you're better than everyone in the room at almost everything, and it will be true, and it will be the most dangerous thing that's ever happened to you, because from then on the fastest way to get anything done correctly will always, always be to do it yourself."

"Yes," said Kate quietly.

"And you'll be very careful about it. You'll have a — what do you call them now — an operating rhythm. You'll have a document. And you'll put in a review, because there was an incident, and the review will be temporary." He glanced sideways. "Was there an incident?"

"There was a payments migration."

"There's always a payments migration." He shifted in the chair. "And it will work. That's the bit nobody tells you. It works. Everything gets better. The numbers get better, the board gets happier, the thing gets bigger. And what you will not be able to see from inside it is that every time you put your hand on something, you take one unit of judgement off somebody else, and they don't grow it back, and eventually you're running a company of two hundred very capable people who all wait."

Kate said nothing.

"And then you're forty-nine, and there's a Sunday night in November where you're on a call at eleven o'clock about a thing that happened in a room you weren't in, and you'll think — " He stopped.

"What?"

"*I should have been there,*" said Michael.

Kate turned her head.

"How do you know that sentence?"

"Because it's mine," said Michael.

---

He said it lightly. That was what she noticed. He said it in exactly the voice he had used the night before to describe closing four hundred branches, and then he did not follow it with a joke, and the absence of the joke was so loud that Kate found herself holding her breath.

"Right," he said. "You asked me what happens at the end. I'm going to tell you, and then we're going to talk about something else, because I have a rule about this and I've already broken it twice this weekend."

"You don't have to."

"I know I don't have to."

He turned the conference programme over.

"You're looking at it," he said. "That's what happens. I'm what it looks like. I have been extremely successful for forty-two years, at some cost — and the cost was fine, I want to be clear, I'd have paid it twice. I have a wife who's still there, and a daughter who's fond of me, and a granddaughter who does the piano and a grandson who has a plan. Nobody in my family has ever been frightened of a letter. That's what I bought. It was worth having."

"But."

"But I optimised everything except the part that was the point," said Michael. "I got it all right. Every single decision, taken on its own, at the time, with the information I had — right. I'd take most of them again." He shook his head slowly. "And they add up to a man who's sixty-four, who is deeply respected by four hundred people, who has to be told twice about his own granddaughter's recital before he registers that he wants to go, and who at some point in the last five years stopped being consulted about anything that matters and has been too proud to notice out loud."

The strip lights hummed. Below them, somebody won something.

"I'm not sad," Michael said. "That's the thing people get wrong. Sad would be a great deal easier — sad you can do something with. I'm just extremely well organised, and it's four o'clock, and I don't know what to do with the afternoon."

Kate sat with her hands in her lap.

"That's the most frightening thing anybody's ever said to me," she said.

"Yes, well," said Michael. "That's why I've got a rule."

---

They did not talk for a bit, which she found she could do with him.

Then Kate said: "Come and work for me."

"No."

"You haven't heard it."

"I've heard several hundred of them."

"Not as an operator." She had turned round in the chair. "Michael, I've got a board of seven people. Two of them are money, three of them are excellent and busy, Harriet is a force of nature, and there is not one single person on it who has ever *run* anything the size of the thing I'm running. They have all governed. None of them has ever had a Sunday night."

Michael was quiet.

"I want you as an advisor," said Kate. "Not a NED — I don't want you in the governance, I don't want you liable for anything, I don't want you reading a hundred-page pack at eleven at night. I want to be able to ring you. That's it. That's the whole job. Four board meetings a year if you can be bothered, and a phone that answers."

"You'd be paying for a phone call."

"I'd be paying for the answer to *since when*," said Kate. "Which I got last night for a gin and tonic, and which is going to save me on Sunday morning, and which I could not have got from anybody I work with, or anybody who works for me, or anybody on that board, because every single one of them would have been forming a view about me while I asked."

There was a long pause.

"That," said Michael, "is a very well constructed argument, and I notice that you have made it without once using the words *mentor* or *journey*, and I'm grateful."

"Is that a yes?"

"It's a — " He looked down at the chess. "Can I tell you the actual reason I'm hesitating? It isn't the time, I've got nothing but time, that's the joke. It's that I have been *rung* for eleven years. There's a phrase people use. 'Only Michael can sign that.' 'We'll need Michael in the room.' And I would like to tell you that I found it wearing, and I did, and I also — " His mouth did something. "There's a thing that happens to a man when the phone stops. And I have spent about two years now pretending that I'd stopped answering it, when in fact it had stopped ringing, and those are extremely different, and I have never said that out loud in my life."

Kate did not say anything at all, which was, at that moment, the hardest and best thing she did all weekend.

"Yes," said Michael. "All right. Yes."

"Really?"

"On one condition, which is that you never once ring me to make you feel better. If you want to be told you're doing well, ring your mother."

Kate laughed, and the laugh had a crack down the middle of it that neither of them chose to look at.

"My mother," she said, "is not really in that line of work."

"No," said Michael. "Mine wasn't either."

---

He said the last thing on the stairs, as she was going down and he was not.

"Kate."

She stopped four steps below him.

"Your problem last night," he said. "The number. That was the first thing in about five years that somebody wanted *me* for and not the job." He had his hand on the rail. "I'd like it noted that I know the difference. That's all."

Then he made a joke about the chair, and she laughed, and went down.

She went into a dinner with seven board members and was, for three hours, delightful. Harriet Nash said, over the coffee, "You seem well," and Kate said, "I've had four hours' sleep and I feel wonderful, which is medically concerning," and Harriet laughed, and Kate went up to eleven at half past eleven, and read the deck twice, and slept for six hours without waking once, which had not happened since March.

And on the fourth floor, Michael Halloway sat on the end of a hotel bed and rang Ruth and told her about a woman who had offered him a job that consisted of a telephone, and Ruth said, "And what did you say?" and Michael said, "I said yes," and there was a pause on the line, and Ruth said, "Michael," in a voice he had not heard in some years, and then said she had to go because there was something in the oven, which there was not.
