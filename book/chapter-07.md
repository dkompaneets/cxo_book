# 7. Post-Mortem

Round one began at seven, and by twenty past seven the atrium of The Ardenne had gone quiet in a way that no other kind of gathering ever manages.

It was not silence. There was the hum of the air handling, a chair, somebody's stomach, and every ninety seconds or so the small plastic *tock* of a clock being pressed. But a hundred and seventy-six people had stopped talking simultaneously and stayed stopped, and the effect on a man arriving from a world of open-plan offices and video calls and a three-year-old was roughly that of stepping into cold water.

Nik sat down at board thirty-one and set out his pieces and felt his shoulders come down two inches.

This was the thing. Not the winning; he had tried to explain the winning to Meera once and had heard himself and stopped. The thing was that for the next four hours nobody was going to say *sorry?* Nobody was going to lean in. There was no accent here. His opponent — a boy of about nineteen with a fringe and a Lidl bag — would not form a view about Nikhil Raghavan's competence based on the first four seconds of a sentence, because there would be no sentences. There would be a position, and the position would be true.

He shook hands. He played e4. The boy played c5.

---

It was not a good game.

Nik got a slight edge out of the opening, the way he usually did, because he had prepared and the boy had not. And then on move seventeen the boy did something loose on the queenside — a pawn push that was, Nik saw immediately, over-ambitious and probably losing.

And Nik did not take it.

He looked at the position for twenty-eight minutes. There was a line — he could see the first six moves of it, clean as a rail — where he opened the centre and his rook came to d7 and it was, if not winning, then extremely uncomfortable to be the boy. It required calculating a knight sacrifice four moves in. He got to the fourth move and could not see the end of it, and the thing that happened next was so familiar that he could have narrated it.

He thought: *and if it doesn't work.*

And then a small piece of machinery turned over inside him, and produced, as it had been producing for twenty years, the alternative: the solid move. Rook to c1. Nothing wrong with it. Kept everything defended. Kept the small edge. Kept the position closed and safe and — this was the honest word, the one he used only in his own head — *unexposed.*

He played Rc1.

He won anyway. It took another fifty-one moves and it was, by the end, an entirely mechanical thing: the boy overpressed at move forty-two out of frustration at not being able to get anything going, and dropped a pawn, and then another, and resigned at move sixty-eight in a completely dead position with a good grace that made Nik feel obscurely ashamed.

"Well played," said the boy.

"You were fine," said Nik. "The pawn on move seventeen."

"I know. I knew as soon as I let go."

They shook hands. It was ten past eleven.

---

The analysis room was the Kenilworth, a function room off the atrium with a carpet from 1998 and fourteen tables of people replaying games at speed, and a tea urn, and a plate of biscuits that the hotel had put out at seven and that had reached the state biscuits reach.

Nik took his score sheet in because that is what you do.

At the far table an old man was sitting alone with a board already set up, moving pieces with one hand, and when Nik came in he looked up and said:

"Raghavan. Board thirty-one."

Nik stopped.

"You beat me," Nik said. "Two years ago. Round four."

"I know."

"You had a rook on the seventh and I—"

"You had a rook on the seventh," said Wilf Tanner, "and you offered a draw, and I refused it, and then I lost. Sit down."

Wilf Tanner was eighty-three. He had been a structural engineer in Coventry for forty-one years and had played tournament chess since 1958, and he had the particular build of a very old man who has stayed exactly the same height, and hands that shook slightly except when they were moving a piece, at which point they did not shake at all.

Nik sat down. He set up.

It took twenty minutes to get to move seventeen, and Wilf did not say a single thing on the way. He simply put the pieces where they had been, in silence, with his eyes on the board, and Nik found himself narrating a little, apologetically — "he'd been quite quick up to here" — and being met with nothing at all, which taught him quickly to stop.

At move seventeen Wilf stopped.

"There," he said.

"I know."

"What did you look at?"

"Knight takes on d5, and then if he recaptures, rook to d7."

"How long?"

"Twenty-eight minutes."

"And you played Rc1."

"I couldn't see the end of it."

Wilf nodded slowly. Then he did a thing that nobody in Nik's professional life had done in six years: he sat, and thought about the answer, and did not fill the gap with anything at all. Thirty seconds went by. Someone at the next table said "no, no, no, *bishop*" and there was laughter.

"Show me," said Wilf. "Knight takes d5. Go on."

Nik played it. Wilf recaptured. Nik played Rd7. Wilf found the defence — the only defence, the one that involved giving back the exchange — and Nik played the follow-up, and Wilf played the second-only-defence, and by the eighth move of a line Nik had not been able to see the end of at the board, they arrived at a position where Wilf had two pawns and a rook against a knight and bishop, and Nik was so plainly winning that neither of them said so.

"Ah," said Nik.

"Yes."

"I mean, at the board—"

"At the board you had twenty-eight minutes," Wilf said, "and it took us four."

Nik looked at the position.

"Right," he said. "So I'm a coward."

He said it lightly, in the tone he used for things like this, the little self-deprecating shrug he had been deploying for two decades and which had never once failed to make an English person say *oh, no, not at all.*

Wilf did not say *oh, no, not at all.* He put the pieces back to move seventeen, and set the position out again neatly, and looked at it rather than at Nik.

"No," he said. "You're not a coward. A coward would have offered a draw." He straightened a rook a fraction. "You won the game. You'll win the next one too, probably, if you get white."

"But?"

"You've got the best defensive technique of anyone in this room under seventy," said Wilf. "Genuinely. It's a real thing, you've built it, it's taken years. And you use it in positions where there's nothing to defend."

Nik said nothing.

"That's not the seventeenth move," Wilf went on. "That's every game of yours I've ever seen. You play like a man guarding a door long after the room behind it has emptied out."

Somebody dropped a chess clock at the next table and there was a general noise.

Wilf reached for a biscuit, found it was one of the bad ones, and put it back.

"So what is it you think you're protecting?" he said.

And Nikhil Raghavan opened his mouth to answer.

It was — he understood this afterwards, upstairs, at half past midnight, lying on the bed with his shoes on — the tone that did it. If the old man had asked it kindly, with his head on one side, in the voice people use when they have decided you are a bit sad, Nik would have had four answers ready, one of them funny. If he had asked it as a challenge, Nik would have had a different four.

But Wilf Tanner had asked it the way he had said everything else, which was flatly, as a technical question about a position, in the manner of a man who has spent forty-one years asking *what is this column actually carrying* and has learned that the answer is very often *nothing, and it has been in the way since 1974.*

He wanted the load path. He was not interested in Nik.

"I don't—" said Nik.

He stopped.

He sat in a function room called the Kenilworth with his hands flat on the table on either side of a chessboard, and went looking, and found that there was nothing there. Not a secret. Not a wound with a story attached to it that he could produce and be understood about. Just a habit with the shape of a room, and the room was empty, and it had been empty for some time, and he was standing in the doorway of it with his hands out.

"That's a very good question," he said at last, in the wrong voice, the board voice, the one that meant *I am going to come back to you on that.*

Wilf Tanner nodded, entirely satisfied, and began putting the pieces away.

"Round two's at two," he said. "Don't eat the fish."
