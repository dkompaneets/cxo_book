# 5. The Third Time

There is a moment in the setting of concrete, the engineers say, when it is still technically workable and no longer in any real sense wet.

---

Kate's phone went at 11:06 on the Sunday, and it was Tom Ferreira, which meant it was real, because Tom did not ring at eleven for practice.

"We've turned off Marrowbone in Germany," he said.

"Since when?"

"Forty minutes. Their step-up authentication's failing about one in nine on German cards and we didn't see it because our alerting's on the aggregate and Germany's eleven per cent of volume."

"How long's it been going on?"

A pause, which was the answer.

"Since Thursday," said Tom.

She was already standing. Thursday, Friday, Saturday, Sunday — the whole weekend, the biggest of the month — and the number came out of her instantly, somewhere between a hundred and forty and a hundred and eighty thousand pounds, and she put it down and went to work.

They fixed it by two in the morning. It was a good piece of work by four tired people; Priyanka found the actual cause, and Tom ran it, and Kate did almost none of it. What Kate did was be there. And at ten past two, alone in the flat, she opened the operating rhythm document and looked at the two review layers she had added a fortnight before, and thought, with the flat clarity that comes at the end of a night like that: *it isn't enough.*

She added a third.

Then on the Tuesday she did something she had never done in nine years at Bellwether, which was to ask for a decision to be re-taken.

It was small — the warehouse in Kettering, whether the new pick-face layout went in during August or after peak. Operations had decided August; the decision was two weeks old; the racking was ordered. Kate got the ops director on a call and said, pleasantly, "Walk me through it."

He walked her through it. It was a good decision. It was so obviously a good decision that by the sixth minute she was no longer listening to the content and was listening instead to the thing underneath it — the sound of a competent man in his fifties explaining to his chief executive, in careful ascending detail, why he had been permitted to do his job.

"Fine," she said. "Do it. I just wanted to understand it."

"No, no. Always happy." And then, in the friendly way people say the thing that costs you most: "Do you want me on the racking schedule? I can put you on the distribution."

*Yes*, said the whole of her body.

"God, no," said Kate. "You don't need me on that."

She thought about it for the rest of the afternoon anyway, and what she thought was: *if I'd been on that distribution I'd have known.* And she watched herself think it, and watched the noticing change nothing at all, and understood, in the ordinary light of a Tuesday, that a belief had finished forming in her at some point in the last six weeks and that she was now simply someone who held it.

The belief was: *anything I am not personally holding will eventually be dropped.*

It was not a mood. She had had moods. This had the texture of arithmetic — the payments review, the German cards, Ocean in March, the returns copy in 2023: four times she had not been in the room, four things gone wrong. Nobody could have argued her out of it, and nobody tried, because she never said it aloud, because saying it aloud would have insulted two hundred people who worked very hard. So it stayed inside, and did what such things do inside, and grew a small orderly root system into every hour of her week.

That evening she declined a dinner with a woman who ran a much larger business and had been trying to have dinner with her since February.

Alex found out about the customer call the same Sunday, by accident, in the most boring way imaginable.

He was tidying the shared drive because he could not face the compliance module, and he opened a folder called *Meridian* and there was a file in it four days old, `Meridian — follow up`, with Ben's initials on it, from a Wednesday on which Alex had sat eleven feet from Ben all day and heard nothing about a call.

He read it. It was ordinary. Meridian wanted the report, Meridian was happy, Meridian had asked about multi-tenancy and Ben had said "on the roadmap."

He texted Ben. *saw the meridian notes — was that a call? did I miss an invite?*

Ben replied inside two minutes, on a Sunday, because Ben always replied. *Ah mate sorry, Suraj rang me direct, 15 min thing, wasn't worth pulling you out of the fix. Should have told you. My bad.* And then, forty seconds later: *Genuinely my bad. Won't happen.*

It was fine. It was obviously fine — a short inbound call to the CEO on a day Alex was buried in a fix, the apology instant and unasked-for. *no worries at all*, Alex typed, and sent it, and then sat for a long time in a flat in Bow, because underneath the entirely reasonable exchange was a thing he could not photograph: that this was the fourth time, that each individual time had been fine, and that he was starting to feel himself routed around the way water routes around a rock — not maliciously, not even deliberately, just because it was faster. There was no way to say *I have a feeling* to a man with a runway ending in February.

He had not opened the notebook in eighteen days. The ideas had not stopped — that was the frightening part; on the Central line on the Monday he had had one about batching the syscall audit that was worth about four months of latency work, and it had arrived whole, the way they did, slightly to one side of what he had been thinking about. He had reached for the pocket.

And thought: *what's the point.*

And by Liverpool Street it had gone the way they go, leaving only the knowledge that there had been one.

Michael's family calendar was a shared one Ruth had set up in 2019 and that he had never once added anything to.

There it was, in the pale blue that meant Claire: *Iris — piano recital, St Barnabas, 6.30, Sat 27th.* He knew at once that he wanted to go — that was not the difficulty. The difficulty was what his mind did next, which was to run, faster than thought, a check against the twenty-seventh, and find nothing there, and *still* not put himself down.

He sat with the remote and worked out why. In September there had been Sam's assembly, and he had put himself in the calendar on the Sunday, and on the Tuesday night Group Risk had blown up, and at a quarter past eleven on the Wednesday morning he had sent Claire the message, and it had been fine, everyone understood, Claire sent a photograph and three words. But there existed, in the world, a seven-year-old boy who had looked at a row of chairs. And Michael had found a way to be safe from that, which was not to be in the row at all: if he did not put himself down, nobody expected him, and coming was a bonus and not-coming was nothing. He had described this to himself for years as *not over-promising*.

Ruth came in with tea and looked at the screen over his shoulder. "You're not on it."

"There's a chance the Ardenne thing runs over."

"The Ardenne thing's on the Monday."

"They do a follow-up. There's usually a follow-up."

Ruth put the tea on the coaster, and looked at the back of his head for a moment, and said, "Right," and went out.

On the Wednesday he heard his own laugh from the outside.

It was in the atrium at lunchtime, with four from Treasury, and somebody did the line about the CEO and the boat and Michael supplied the follow-up he had been carrying for a fortnight, and the four of them went up like a struck match. And Michael laughed too, and heard it — coming from somewhere out to his left, about a foot away, a bark with a rise on the end, a completely convincing noise made by a professional — and stood in the middle of the atrium with a plate in his hand and thought, with total detachment: *whose laugh is that.*

The group broke up. Vanessa Okoro stayed behind and said, in a different register: "How are you, though?"

There are perhaps four people in any organisation who will ask that with the *though* on the end, which is the load-bearing word; the *though* means *I have noticed something and I am giving you a door.* She was in a hurry and she had stayed behind to use it.

Michael took a small breath.

"Well," he said, "I'm about to write four hundred words on why banking is meaningful, so — physically I'm fine, spiritually I'm on a ventilator."

Vanessa laughed. She really laughed; she put a hand on his arm. "Send it to me. I'll tell you if it's rubbish."

"You'll tell me it's marvellous and then not come."

"That is exactly what I'll do," she said, delighted, and went.

And Michael stood there holding the plate. It had taken about a second and a half, and he had done it without deciding to, and the extraordinary thing — the thing he turned over on the train that evening and could not put down — was that he had felt *relief*. Physical relief, at the sound of her laughing. As if the danger had not been the tiredness but the possibility of somebody looking at it.

They rang Nik at 9:40, which was past two in the morning in Chennai, which meant his parents had waited up.

"Nikhil. Listen — the hospital wants the whole thing in advance. Not just the operation. The physiotherapy, twelve weeks, they've put it in a package. Appa says it's too much."

"How much."

"One lakh sixty more."

He did it before she finished — fifteen hundred and twenty pounds, plus the fee, plus the spread they took and did not print.

"That's fine," said Nik.

"It's not fine, it's—"

"Amma. It's genuinely fine. Things are good here. I got a bonus in June." He had not got a bonus in June. The words simply arrived, complete, in the right voice, and stepped out of him the way they had for twenty years. "I'll send it Tuesday."

"A bonus!" His mother's voice went up like a bird. "Raghava, he got a bonus." And in the background his father, so quiet Nik could barely catch it, saying something that was probably *don't tell everyone*.

He put the phone down. Meera came in for a glass of water and said, in the flat voice of a woman on four hours' sleep who is not accusing anyone of anything, "Anaya's school fees went up. It's eleven-forty now, not ten-eighty — there was a letter in May. First of August, the next one. Just so it's not a surprise."

"It won't be a surprise," said Nik.

She went up. And on the Saturday, with Rohan at his grandmother's and Anaya at a trampoline party and the house empty — which happened about six times a year — he made a coffee and sat down at the kitchen table and opened *2020 household v3*, because he was going to do this properly.

He updated the income tab. He updated the fixed costs. He made a new row for Chennai and called it *one-off* and typed two numbers into it and looked at the row title and knew perfectly well what a category called *one-off* containing two entries is going to be next year.

Then he opened the credit card statement, because to do it properly he had to bring the card in.

He looked at the balance for about forty seconds. It was not catastrophic; that was somehow worse. It was a number a man in his position should not have, arrived at in increments of two and three hundred pounds over fourteen months, every one of which he could have named, and none of which had been extravagant.

And he found that he could not type it into the cell.

Not *would not*. Could not. He sat with his hands on the keys in an empty house on a Saturday morning, and there was a physical block between the number on one screen and the empty cell on the other, and he understood that if he typed it in, the model would recalculate, and the twelve-month view at the bottom would turn, and then it would be true in writing.

He closed the tab. He drank the coffee, which had gone cold, and said out loud, to the empty kitchen:

"I'll do it at the hotel."

It sounded completely reasonable. He would have five days of quiet and evenings in a room with nothing in them. He would do it properly, with time, the way it deserved.

He saved the file, and closed it.

And in four kitchens, in four cities, in the same week, four people put something down where they could reach it later.

Then they packed.
