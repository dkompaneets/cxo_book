# 13. The Old Country

They arrived at twenty past ten on the Saturday morning, having got a train from Euston at 7:41 to be safe, and then a second train to be safe, and then a taxi, and having therefore been travelling since a quarter past six for a journey of ninety minutes.

Ewa Wójcik was wearing the coat.

"Mama, it's twenty-four degrees."

"It's a coat for arriving," said his mother.

Marek Wójcik was sixty-eight and had a bad shoulder from the print works and carried both bags, and when Alex tried to take one there was a short, complete, entirely wordless struggle in the lobby of The Ardenne which Marek won, as he had won it every time since 2009.

They checked in. It took a while, because Ewa wanted to know about breakfast and then wanted to know about breakfast again in a different way, and because Marek stood four feet back from the desk with the bags and watched the transaction with the total, unblinking attention of a man who has been overcharged in three countries.

Then Ewa went up to look at the room, and to unpack, which would take forty minutes and could not be hurried, and Alex and his father sat down in two chairs by the fireplace with no fire in it.

"So," said Marek.

"So."

"It's a big hotel."

"It's a big hotel."

His father looked up at the nine hundred glass rods for a while.

"Twenty-nine pounds," he said.

"There was an offer."

"Mm," said Marek.

---

Nik came through the lobby at eleven on his way to the atrium, and Alex saw him and did the thing you do, the half-lift of the hand that gives the other person a way out.

Nik did not take the way out. He came over.

"Round three's at two," he said, "so I'm free for exactly two hours and then I'm no use to anybody."

"This is my dad. Papa — this is Nik. He beat me at chess in nineteen moves."

Marek Wójcik stood up, which took him a second, and shook hands, and said:

"Nineteen. Is good. When he was ten he was — how you say — he was crying if it's more than thirty."

"*Papa.*"

"Is true."

And that was it: forty seconds, and the two of them had found it.

Alex sat and watched it happen and could not have said afterwards where the turn was. Somewhere in the first four minutes his father said *when we came*, and Nik said *when did you come*, and Marek said 1994, and Nik said 1999, and there was a small pause, and then the two of them were off in a country that Alex had lived beside his whole life and had never once been able to enter.

They did not talk about hardship. That was the part that undid Alex, and he thought about it for months. He had assumed, insofar as he had ever imagined this conversation, that it would be a comparing of wounds. It was not. It was almost entirely logistics, and it was very funny.

They talked about the first flat. Marek's had been in Leyton, two rooms, and had had a meter that took fifty-pence pieces, and the landlord had been a man called Costas who was, Marek said, "not bad, but he was thinking always I am stealing the fifty pence from my own meter." Nik's had been his uncle's box room in Leicester with a wardrobe he could not fully open because of the bed.

They talked about the documents. Both of them, it emerged, had a plastic wallet. Marek's was blue and lived in a drawer in the kitchen and had done for thirty-one years and contained every piece of paper the Home Office had ever sent him, in order, including the envelopes. Nik's was clear and lived in a fireproof box, and when he admitted the fireproof box Marek nodded slowly and said, "Yes. Yes. I think about this," in the tone of a man conceding a technical point.

And they talked about the language.

"The words was fine," Marek said. "Words you learn. Is the — " He made a gesture, a small quick circling with two fingers. "The speed. They talk and you have the words, all of them, but they finish, and you are still — " he moved the fingers again "— you are still one sentence behind. And then they look."

"And then they look," said Nik.

"So you say nothing."

"So you say nothing."

"For me was six years," said Marek. "Six years I say nothing. At the works there is a man, Tony, he think I am — he think there is something wrong with me. Six years! Then one day the machine is broken and I fix it, and everybody is standing there, and Tony says, 'Marek, you're a dark horse.'" He shrugged with the good shoulder. "Twenty years we are friends. He came to Alek's christening. But six years."

---

Nik watched him.

He watched him for the whole two hours, in fact, and would have been unable to explain why he did not go and prepare for round three.

Marek Wójcik was sixty-eight. He had come over at thirty-seven with a wife, a four-year-old, and a trade that did not transfer. He had done nights at a bakery on Bethnal Green Road and days at a print works for ten years, which Nik had done the arithmetic on within about ninety seconds of hearing it — ten years of four hours' sleep — and he had bought a flat in 2006 and had paid it off in 2021 and was now retired and had a bad shoulder and had come to Birmingham on two trains to see his son for a weekend.

And he was *calm*.

That was the thing. Nik kept looking for it and could not find it. He knew what he was looking for, because he carried it: the low hum under everything, the running total, the eye that goes to the price on the menu first. He had assumed — without ever putting it into a sentence — that it was simply what this life did to a man, and that in thirty years he would be a version of himself with more money and the same hum, and that this was the deal.

Marek Wójcik did not have the hum.

He had an opinion about the coffee. He had a long and detailed complaint about a train company. He asked Nik four questions about Chennai and listened to all four answers. When Ewa came down and said the room was fine but the pillows were "like a paper", he said "so we ask for another pillow," and Nik watched a man solve a problem in four words at a volume of about thirty per cent, and understood, with a jolt that he covered by drinking some water, that this was what it looked like when the door had actually been shut and the room behind it was actually empty and the man had walked away from it.

*The fear ends*, Nik thought. Not: the fear was never justified. It had been justified; six years of saying nothing at a print works in Leyton is not a misunderstanding.

It ends.

He had genuinely, at forty-two, not known that this was available.

---

Alex, sitting slightly outside the conversation, watched his father through a stranger's eyes and had his own private demolition.

He knew what he had always felt in this situation. He had catalogued it at eleven, at fourteen, at nineteen, at every parents' evening and every school gate and every one of the four times his father had come into the Velum office: a hot, tight, contemptible, entirely automatic wish that his father would say the sentence *slightly differently.*

*The words was fine.* Was. He had heard it. Of course he had heard it; he heard every one.

And nothing happened.

He sat in a lobby chair with a coffee going cold and waited for the flinch and it did not come, because the flinch had never been about the grammar. The flinch had been about what he assumed the other person was thinking, and the other person here was a chief financial officer with two children and a chess rating who was leaning forward with his elbows on his knees, listening to Marek Wójcik the way Alex had watched people listen at conferences to men with much better English and far less to say.

His father was interesting. That was all. His father had always been interesting and Alex had spent twenty years hearing him as a mistake.

At about half past twelve Ewa took a photograph of the three of them without asking, badly, at an angle, and it is the photograph that would eventually sit on a shelf in a flat in Bow.

---

They got round to it because Nik asked, plainly, the way he had asked about everything else: "So how is it going, the company?"

And Alex, who had said *it's great* to these people on the phone eleven days ago, said, "It's — there's a thing with my co-founder."

He told it in about four minutes, badly, in the version that made everybody reasonable: the whiteboard, the compliance module, the twenty-two seconds of silence, the fact that Ben was a good man, the fact that there was nothing you could photograph.

Marek listened without moving.

At the end he said, "Is he a liar?"

"No. Genuinely no."

"Is he stupid?"

"He's the least stupid person I know."

"Mm." Marek looked at the fireplace with no fire in it. "So he is fast. And you are — " He hunted, and did not find the word in English, and used the Polish one, and Alex translated it in his head as something between *thorough* and *slow*, and it was accurate, and it was not an insult. "So. He is fast and you are that. And the room goes to who is fast."

"Yes."

"Yes," said his father. "This I know."

He turned in the chair.

"Aleksander." He said it to the fireplace rather than to his son. "You don't have to be loud to be right."

Alex looked at the floor.

"That doesn't help, though," he said, more sharply than he meant to. "That's a nice thing to say, but the room *does* go to who's fast. Being right quietly is just being wrong slowly."

"I didn't finish," said Marek.

"Sorry."

His father took a while over it. He was not building to anything; he was looking for words in a language that had never entirely fitted him.

"You say it one time," he said. "Slow. Then you stop, and you don't say it again." A pause. "If you say it one time and stop, they have to look at it. Three times, it is noise." He turned his hand over. "And if you don't say it, then it was never in the room. And after, you are angry with them for a thing they never saw."

Two feet away, Nikhil Raghavan sat absolutely still.

"At the works I said nothing, six years," Marek went on, to nobody in particular. "Then the machine was broken."

---

Nik went off to round three at twenty to two and lost, comprehensively, to a man he should have beaten, because he was not there.

Marek and Ewa and Alex had lunch in the restaurant, and Ewa asked about the food four times, and Marek asked about the compliance module and was told about it and understood roughly none of it and asked two further questions anyway.

And at the end, when Ewa had gone to find the toilets, Marek Wójcik said, looking at his son across a table:

"You are all right?"

And Alex said, "Yeah. Tired. It's a big year."

And his father — who had spent thirty-one years, four hours of sleep at a time, on exactly one thing in this world, and who had known since the moment he walked into that lobby that something was badly wrong with his son, and had watched him all morning, and had counted the number of times he checked his phone, and had heard the eleven days of *it's great* — looked at him for a second and a half.

"Good," he said. "Then we go and look at this canal your mother has read about."
