# 25. The Recital

The advisory arrangement with Bellwether went through in the middle of August, on a single sheet of paper that Michael read twice and signed once, and which committed him to four board meetings a year and "such counsel as may reasonably be sought by telephone," for a fee that he thought was too high and that Kate refused to lower.

And on the twenty-first of August, Michael Halloway told Lauriston he would retire at the end of March.

He told Fiona first, in her office, with the door shut. She did not perform surprise, which he was grateful for. She said, "Is this because of the transformation-office thing? Because I can tell you that Callum's not—" and Michael said, "No. It's genuinely not. It's that I'm sixty-four and I've been doing this since Thatcher and I taught four hundred people about the 1994 ledger in July and a woman in row four asked me a question and it was the best afternoon I'd had in a decade, and I'd like more afternoons and fewer mornings." Which was true, and was not the whole of it, and Fiona — who had heard about a search from somebody, because people always have — let him have the version he offered, because she was decent, and because it was, as far as it went, real.

He did not mention Terry's phone call. He never mentioned it to anybody. In October a mid-size building society announced a new group operations director, an external hire, early fifties, big transformation background, and Michael read the announcement on his phone on the 6:40 and felt the thing settle into place with a click, like a bone going back, and put the phone in the door pocket, and looked out of the window, and it was — he checked — genuinely all right. It had been going to be somebody. It had turned out to be somebody at a building society and not at Lauriston, and by then it did not matter, because by then he had already jumped, and a man who has already jumped cannot be pushed.

His calendar, in September, began to acquire white space.

It terrified him for exactly one week. There was a Wednesday afternoon, the second week, with nothing in it — actually nothing, a blank rectangle from two o'clock — and he sat in his office and looked at it and felt the old animal panic, the one from Bushey, the one that said *a man with an empty afternoon is a man without a wage.* He very nearly filled it. He got as far as opening the diary of a colleague to offer to sit in on something.

Then he closed it, and went home at two o'clock on a Wednesday for the first time since 1987, and Ruth was not expecting him, and there was a moment at the door where she thought something was wrong, and then there wasn't, and they went for a walk.

It was on one of those afternoons that he rang the boy. He had been carrying the last night at the hotel around for weeks — *he's a mascot*, and the rest of it, all accurate — and he got the voicemail, and was slightly relieved, because he had not fully worked out what he meant to say.

"Alex. It's Michael. From the — from the hotel. The mascot." A pause, and something that was almost the joke, and then not. "Listen. You were right about most of it, and I've thought about it a great deal, and I wanted to say so, and I wanted to say that the introduction — I should have sent it to you. Just you. You were right about that, and I knew the reason while I was doing it, and you named it, and it's cost me some sleep, which at my age is a genuine sacrifice." A breath. "Anyway. I hear the recon thing worked. Of course it did. Ring me back if you want to. And if you don't, that's — I'd understand that too. Right. That's the message. I'd say don't let it go to your head, but I've met your head, so — too late. Goodbye."

Alex was, that week, in the middle of a release and a fight with Saul about resourcing, and he listened to it on a Thursday, standing up, half-hearing it, and thought *I'll ring him back at the weekend*, and meant it, and was still angry enough that the weekend was a convenient distance away.

---

The recital was on a Saturday in late September, at St Barnabas Hall, and Michael went unannounced.

He had thought about it carefully, which was itself the tell. The old Michael would have rung Claire and said *is it all right if I come*, and would have offered to sit at the back, and would have made a joke about Rufus, and would thereby have converted his own attendance into a small favour that the family granted him, so that if anything went wrong he would have been the man who was kind enough to offer rather than the man who failed to come.

He did not do that. He simply drove to Watford, to a church hall four miles from the maisonette in Bushey where a man had once put a sticker on a television, and he arrived at 6:10 for a 6:30 start, and he stood at the back because the four seats were, as promised, taken.

Claire saw him from across the hall and her face did something complicated, and she came over, and said, "Dad, you didn't say—" and Michael said, "No. I didn't. I wanted to just be here rather than be a whole thing about being here," and Claire looked at him for a second and said, "Right. OK," and then, "There's a chair by the radiator, it's the worst seat in the room," and Michael said, "It's a magnificent seat," and went and sat on it.

Iris played third. She was nine, and she played a piece by someone Michael had never heard of, and she got the fingering wrong in one place and did the thing good children do, which is to not stop, to keep the left hand going and let the right hand find its way back, and Michael, on a plastic chair by a radiator in a church hall in Watford, discovered that he was not able to see very well.

A boy called Rufus did indeed play "Für Elise" at a speed that suggested a wager.

---

Afterwards there was squash and biscuits, and Iris came and found him, in the specific way children do when they know they have done a thing, and Michael said, "You went wrong in the middle bit," and Iris said, "I *know*," and Michael said, "and then you did the cleverest thing I've seen all year, which is that you kept the other hand going and you didn't apologise, you just fixed it while nobody was looking, and that, Iris, is the entire secret of every job I have ever had, and you did it at nine, and I'm extremely proud of you."

Iris considered this.

"Grandad, are you crying?"

"Yes," said Michael. "It's the squash. It's very strong squash."

And it was at that point, with a plastic cup of squash and his granddaughter's hand on his sleeve, that Claire, standing a little way off, watching, put her own hand over her mouth for a second, because she had not seen her father cry in her entire life, and had assumed, in the way you assume things about a parent, that he could not.

---

He said it to her on the porch afterwards, when the others had gone in.

"Claire. I got it wrong."

"Dad—"

"No, let me. I've been practising and if I don't do it now I'll do the joke." He put the cup down on the windowsill. "I bought you and your brother everything. Every single thing. There was never a letter in that house that anyone was frightened of, and I did that on purpose, and I'd do it again. But I bought it with the only thing I actually had to give you, which was the time, and I sold you the time to buy you the things, and now the things are here and the time is gone, and I can't get it back. I know I can't get it back. I'm not asking you to say it's fine." He looked at her. "I missed it. Most of it. And I'm sorry."

Claire was quiet.

She did not say *it's fine, Dad*, and Michael loved her for it more than he had ever managed to tell her, because she was the one person who did not reach for the thing that would make the moment easier.

"It wasn't fine," she said. "I want to be honest with you because you're being honest with me. There were — Dad, there were a lot of empty chairs. Sam doesn't even — Sam thinks it's normal that grandparents don't come to things, because yours mostly didn't, and I never corrected it because I didn't want him to feel it as a loss, so I sort of — I priced you out. To protect him. And that was its own thing and I'm not proud of it either."

"No," said Michael. "That's — you were right to."

"I wasn't right. I was coping." She wiped her eye with the back of her wrist. "But you're here. You came to a recital and stood at the back and made my daughter feel like the cleverest person in Watford, and you're crying about squash, and I don't — Dad, I don't know what happened to you at that hotel, but."

"A great many things happened to me at that hotel," said Michael, "and I'll tell you all of them, and one of them was a young man with a marker pen, and one of them was a woman who gave me a job that's a telephone, and one of them was that I said some true things to some strangers that I'll be ashamed of until I die." He picked the cup back up. "But mostly I just stopped, for two days, and it turned out I could see from there."

---

Danny was on the porch when Michael went to leave, having a moment to himself the way a man does when there are twelve people in his house, and Michael knew the look, because he had invented it.

Danny was thirty-eight and worked for a logistics company and was, by every visible sign — the phone, the two-thumbs reply at the child's recital, the specific grey around the eyes — working exactly the way Michael had worked at thirty-eight.

"Long week?" said Michael.

"Aren't they all," said Danny, and laughed, and it was a good laugh, and it had a floor under it, and Michael recognised the floor.

So Michael sat down on the step, which took him a second, and said, "Can I tell you something, and you can ignore it, because I'm your wife's father and this is exactly the sort of thing that is unbearable coming from your wife's father?"

Danny, wary, amused, said, "Go on."

And Michael Halloway took a breath in — Danny watched him do it, a long breath, in through the nose, the way he did before the things that mattered, though Danny did not know that and never would — and let it out, and then he told him.

He told him all of it. Not as a warning, and not as a lesson, and not with a moral on the end, because he had learned in July that a man declines to hear the moral. He just told him, plainly, unedited, without a single joke: the branches, the years, the sticker on the television, the promise a boy made at a table in Bushey, the four hundred branches, the empty chairs, the phone that stopped ringing, the granddaughter he had to be told twice about, the recital he had nearly not come to. He told him it had all been the right decision every single time and that it added up to a thing he could not undo. He talked for eleven minutes on a step in the dark and Danny did not say anything at all, and at the end Michael said:

"I'm not telling you to leave your job. I left mine at sixty-four and made a mess of things I can't fix, so I'm the last man who should be advising anybody." He put his hands on his knees. "Do what you like with it."

Danny sat on the step for a while.

"Iris asks about you a lot," he said eventually. "I don't think you know that."

"No," said Michael. "I didn't."

"Yeah." Danny looked at his hands. "Thanks."

Michael drove home the long way, through Bushey, which he had not done on purpose in thirty years, and did not stop, and did not slow down much either.
